🏟️ College Athletics

NCAA Overhauls Eligibility With New Age-Based "5-Year" Model — and Lawsuits Are Already Flying

The NCAA Division I Cabinet voted on June 23, 2026, to adopt a single age-based eligibility model, eliminating season-of-competition, sport-specific, redshirt, and waiver rules.

Under the new framework, Division I student-athletes receive up to five years of eligibility if they enroll in college no later than the academic year after their 19th birthday.

Already, more than 50 college basketball student-athletes from the 2022 high school graduating class have filed or will file lawsuits arguing that they will not benefit from the potential extra year of eligibility.

NCAA.org — Age-Based Eligibility

July 31 Waiver Deadline Looms as Old Eligibility Rules Expire for Good

For current student-athletes with eligibility remaining under the previous rules, schools must submit any season-of-competition or eligibility clock extension waiver requests no later than July 31, 2026 — after that date, waivers of the previous rules will no longer be available. Athletic compliance offices are racing to meet the hard cutoff before the prior framework is permanently retired. The age-based framework was also embedded in the Protect College Sports Act, a federal bill that has passed the Senate Commerce Committee and awaits a full Senate vote.

NCAA.org — Eligibility Waiver Deadline

College Sports Commission's NIL Enforcement Struggles as August 1 Deadline Approaches

On June 23, 2026, the College Sports Commission announced a significant modification to its enforcement approach: effective July 1, 2026, most Associated NIL deals between $600 and $15,000 will not undergo Range of Compensation review.

The CSC still lacks much enforcement power given that power conference schools have not yet signed the participant agreements that would shield the CSC from litigation — and some NIL collectives have already stopped reporting deals through the system altogether. The August 1 effective date for stricter NIL standards is now just two weeks away, leaving athletic departments and collectives in a state of uncertainty.

Front Office Sports — Year Schools Paid Their Players

Revenue-Sharing Year One: Power 4 Schools Are Maxing Out the $20.5M Cap and Looking for More

Each participating Division I institution may share up to 22% of average revenue derived from media rights, ticket sales, and sponsorships across Power Five schools — a cap that sits at approximately $20.5 million per school for 2025–26.

Power 4 schools are already interested in finding third-party NIL deals to compensate athletes above the $20.5 million annual cap they're maxing out. The financial pressure is reshaping how athletic directors approach everything from roster construction to booster fundraising heading into the fall season.

Global Chip Selloff Deepens on AI Spending Doubts, Dragging Nasdaq Into Its Worst Week in Months

U.S. stock index futures slid on Friday as a selloff in chip stocks deepened, forcing investors to reassess the staying power of this year's AI-fueled rally — after a blistering run that lifted Wall Street to record highs, investors are retreating from crowded semiconductor trades.

Memory-chip makers fell sharply for a second straight day, with SanDisk, Western Digital, Seagate, and Micron down between 4.6% and 6.5%, while the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index hit a nearly two-month low and was set for its worst week since March 2025.

Netflix Tumbles 8%+ After Revenue Guidance Disappoints and Transparency Pulls Back

Netflix shares fell over 8% in extended trading after the company forecasted a second straight quarter of slowing sales growth.

Netflix earned 80 cents per share on revenue of $12.56 billion — analysts had expected 79 cents on revenue of $12.59 billion — but the company also said it would cut back how often it releases its "What We Watched" reports, which give investors a look at platform engagement.

Oil extended its weekly advance on the escalating conflict, with West Texas Intermediate rising above $79 a barrel and Brent closing near $84 on Thursday — on track for a weekly gain of more than 11%.

Yankees in Advanced Talks With Apollo for Nearly $3 Billion Financing Deal

The New York Yankees are in advanced talks to raise nearly $3 billion of financing from Apollo Global Management, as more institutional capital flows into professional sports.

The MLB giant and Apollo's sports investing unit are discussing a package comprising mostly debt and also some equity.

The deal would mark Apollo's largest investment in U.S. sports to date if it goes through.

The tournament has been accompanied by numerous controversies relating to FIFA ticket pricing, political, logistical, and human rights issues across its three host nations — the United States, Mexico, and Canada. CalPERS Launches $600B "Total Portfolio" Experiment, Scrapping Decades-Old Strategy

Starting July 1, 2026, the total portfolio approach replaced the strategic asset allocation model CalPERS had used to guide investment decision-making.

CalPERS investment chief Stephen Gilmore wants to break down walls between asset classes with "total portfolio" investing. The shift at the nation's largest public pension fund represents a fundamental redesign of how it allocates across stocks, bonds, private equity, and real assets — with implications for every asset manager seeking the fund's capital.

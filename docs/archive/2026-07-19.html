🏟️ College Athletics Business (D1 Ticker Beat)

1. Protect College Sports Act Gains Senate Floor Momentum with New Cosponsors

Just last week, on July 13, Senators Shelley Moore Capito (R-W.Va.) and Peter Welch (D-Vt.) added their names as cosponsors to the Protect College Sports Act, the sweeping bipartisan bill led by Sens. Ted Cruz and Maria Cantwell that aims to establish nationwide standards governing NIL, transfers, eligibility, and enforcement. The Commerce Committee had already advanced the bill with an overwhelming 19–9 vote, sending it to the Senate floor.

As of this week, 24 collegiate athletic conferences and 267 colleges and universities across 49 states have publicly supported the legislation. The full Senate vote remains the central question for college athletics executives entering the fall.

🔗 Senate Commerce Committee — Protect College Sports Act

2. NCAA's New Age-Based Eligibility Model Is Now Law — With a Hard Waiver Deadline Looming

On June 23, the NCAA Division I Cabinet unanimously approved a historic overhaul of eligibility rules, adopting an age-based model granting student-athletes five full seasons of competition after entering college — the most significant structural change to NCAA eligibility rules in decades.

For current student-athletes with eligibility remaining under previous rules, schools must submit any waiver requests to the national office no later than July 31, 2026; after that date, waivers of the old rules will no longer be available. Athletic directors and compliance offices face a hard two-week deadline to act.

🔗 NCAA.org — Division I Adopts Age-Based Eligibility Model

3. CSC Relaxes NIL Clearinghouse Rules for Smaller Deals, Easing a Major Compliance Burden

On June 23, the College Sports Commission announced a significant modification to its enforcement approach: effective July 1, 2026, most associated NIL deals between $600 and $15,000 will not undergo the full Range of Compensation review unless flagged. This is a notable rollback from the original framework that had alarmed smaller-market programs and non-revenue sport collectives. Federal NIL legislation is still unresolved — Congress has debated national NIL rules since 2025, but no bill has passed, and any federal law could change the landscape quickly.

🔗 Fred Law — New College Sports Economy After House

4. House Settlement Back-Pay Damages Still Frozen as Athlete Appeals Grind On

After the settlement was approved, seven separate groups of athletes filed appeals challenging various aspects of it; as a result, the damages payments — which exceed $2.5 billion — will not be paid to athletes while those appeals are pending.

The first payments remain delayed due to Title IX concerns raised by female athletes, and additional litigation is expected as Congress works on legislation to resolve questions that linger even after the settlement's final approval. The freeze continues to cloud the financial planning of athletic departments expecting to draw down settlement funds.

🔗 WilmerHale — Final Approval for House v. NCAA Settlement

5. Chinese AI Startup Moonshot Triggers Chip Bear Market, Upending Wall Street's Biggest Trade

A surprise breakthrough from Chinese startup Moonshot rippled through global markets Friday, sending AI and semiconductor stocks sharply lower and reviving memories of last year's so-called DeepSeek moment — the latest reminder that assumptions about tomorrow's winners can change almost overnight.

The selloff drove the high-profile group of chipmakers into bear market territory, posting their worst week since April 2025 as a key industry gauge sank 20% from a record. Retail investors crowding into leveraged AI funds absorbed especially heavy losses.

🔗 Bloomberg — AI Selloff Hits Leveraged Funds

6. U.S.–Iran Hostilities Escalate, Sending Oil Surging and Safe-Haven Assets Higher

Oil jumped sharply this past week as the U.S. and Iran traded attacks again.

Iran cautioned it would "crush" key regional targets if the U.S. strikes its infrastructure, after Trump said in a Fox News interview that U.S. forces would target key Iranian infrastructure should diplomacy fail.

Traditional safe-haven assets rose in response, with the 10-year U.S. Treasury yield falling to 4.537% and spot gold climbing above $4,000 per ounce.

🔗 Bloomberg — Stock Market Today, July 17

7. Fed Chair Warsh Defends Independence, Signals No Rate Cuts Despite Trump Pressure

Fed Chairman Kevin Warsh stated that President Trump — who has long called for slashing interest rates — has not tried to interfere with the central bank, and would not succeed if he attempted to do so.

Inflation had risen to a three-year high of 4.2% in May, pushed up by the Iran war's impact on gas prices, though a recent peace agreement has caused gas prices to decline, suggesting inflation may have peaked. Warsh has notably shifted from his pre-appointment stance of calling for lower rates, now firmly prioritizing price stability.

🔗 Bloomberg — Warsh Says He's Told Trump 'Repeatedly' He's Independent

8. CalPERS Launches $600 Billion "Total Portfolio" Investment Overhaul

CalPERS CIO Stephen Gilmore runs the $600 billion portfolio of the California Public Employees' Retirement System, the largest pension fund in the U.S.

The fund adopted a Total Portfolio Approach — ditching traditional asset-allocation targets and instead judging every investment on its role within the whole portfolio — with the move taking effect July 1, designed to "increase transparency and give staff more flexibility to capitalize on a variety of market opportunities." The experiment is being closely watched by institutional investors worldwide as a potential model for managing massive, multi-decade liability portfolios.

🔗 Bloomberg — A $600 Billion Experiment Kicks Off at CalPERS

9. Europe Moves to Break America's Grip on Payment Infrastructure

Concerns over economic sovereignty are fueling a European search for alternatives to Visa and Mastercard. Several EU governments and central banks have been backing new domestic and pan-European payment rails as part of a broader strategy to reduce financial dependence on U.S. platforms — a trend accelerating amid ongoing transatlantic trade and geopolitical tensions. The push could reshape billions in annual transaction fee flows and challenge the duopoly's European market share.

🔗 Bloomberg — Europe Fights to Loosen America's Grip on Payment Systems

10. Gen Z Traders Are Treating Meme Stocks and Options Like Lottery Tickets

Lottery-like meme stocks and options are attracting young investors as a perceived way to beat high home prices, inflation, and AI job threats.

Bloomberg Intelligence estimates the share of stock market volume from retail investors has doubled over the past 15 years, with social media and the gamification of trading playing a decisive role in the growth of investing in crypto, options, sports betting, and prediction markets. The AI market selloff this week hit this cohort particularly hard, as leveraged positions in semiconductor and AI-adjacent funds cratered.

🔗 Bloomberg — Gen-Z Traders Go for Broke in Pursuit of a New American Dream

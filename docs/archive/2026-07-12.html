2. NCAA Wipes Out Redshirts With Landmark Age-Based Eligibility Overhaul — July 31 Waiver Deadline Looms

The Division I Cabinet unanimously approved a sweeping overhaul of eligibility rules, permitting Division I student-athletes up to five years of eligibility if they enroll in college no later than the academic year after their 19th birthday; the new rule streamlines a significant portion of the Division I rulebook by eliminating season-of-competition limits, sport-specific eligibility and redshirt rules, and eligibility extension waivers.

For current student-athletes with eligibility remaining under the previous rules, schools must submit any season-of-competition or eligibility clock extension waiver requests to the national office no later than July 31, 2026 — after that date, waivers of the previous rules will no longer be available.

The rule already faces legal pushback, as 15 basketball players from the high school class of 2022 filed a lawsuit shortly after its adoption, alleging that granting additional eligibility to some in the same class but not others is arbitrary and a breach of contractual duties.

II · Today's Book

The Fighter's Mind: Inside the Mental Game

by Sam Sheridan

The Fighter's Mind: Inside the Mental Game — Detailed Key-Ideas Brief

1. Central Thesis and Why It Matters

Sam Sheridan's The Fighter's Mind is built on a deceptively simple premise: the body has limits that training can push, but the mind sets the actual ceiling. In any combat sport — boxing, wrestling, Brazilian jiu-jitsu, mixed martial arts, Muay Thai — two athletes of nearly identical physical conditioning will be separated by the quality of their mental architecture. The fighter who wins is not necessarily the strongest or the fastest; it is the one who has constructed an interior world capable of enduring suffering, sustaining focus under chaos, managing fear without being paralyzed by it, and making clear decisions when the body is screaming for escape.

Sheridan arrives at this thesis not as an armchair theorist but as a working journalist and amateur fighter who embedded himself with coaches, champions, and martial arts scholars across multiple disciplines. He interviews figures as diverse as Dan Gable, the legendary wrestling coach, Randy Couture, the UFC champion who fought well past the age when most competitors retire, and Marcelo Garcia, the Brazilian jiu-jitsu prodigy. Each conversation is a window into a different dimension of mental performance, and together they build a composite portrait of what it actually means to be mentally strong in a domain where the consequences of weakness are immediate and physical.

Why does this matter beyond fighting? Because the fight is a compressed laboratory. In ordinary life, the psychological pressures that sabotage performance — fear, ego, pain avoidance, the desire to quit — are diffuse and slow-moving. In a fight they are immediate and unmistakable. The mental skills that champions develop to survive those pressures — the ability to reframe suffering, to stay present, to manage ego, to think tactically under stress — are directly transferable to any high-pressure human endeavor. Business, medicine, athletics, creative work, and even parenting involve versions of the same interior battles. The fight just makes the lesson undeniable.

2. Major Frameworks, Models, and Arguments

The Pain Threshold Is a Mental Construction

The foundational argument of the book is that pain tolerance is not a fixed biological trait but a trainable cognitive skill. Sheridan documents, through multiple conversations, that elite fighters learn to separate the sensation of pain from the interpretation of pain. Pain perceived as threatening triggers panic, muscle tension, and cognitive shutdown — all of which accelerate defeat. Pain reinterpreted as information, or even as a signal of productive effort, can be metabolized without disrupting performance.

Dan Gable is the most powerful example Sheridan uses here. Gable trained with a ferocity that bordered on self-destruction, building a physical and psychological tolerance for suffering that was simply beyond what his opponents could match. His logic was almost Stoic in structure: if you have already experienced your worst possible scenario in training, competition holds no surprises. The suffering in a match is always less than the suffering you have voluntarily inflicted on yourself in the gym. This creates a kind of psychological surplus — a reserve of confidence grounded not in positive thinking but in demonstrated evidence.

The Ego as Both Engine and Obstacle

One of the book's recurring tensions is the dual role of ego. Every elite fighter Sheridan encounters has a formidable ego — a deep belief in their own capacity and a refusal to accept inferiority. Without that ego, they would never have endured the years of grinding work that produced their skill. Yet that same ego, left unmanaged, becomes a liability. A fighter who cannot accept being submitted in training, who refuses to tap, who will not acknowledge a weakness, cannot learn. The ego that drives them into the gym becomes the thing that prevents them from improving once they are in it.

Sheridan frames this as a fundamental paradox of combat sports: you need enough ego to believe you can win against anybody, but you need enough humility to lose gracefully in practice and extract the lesson. The fighters who navigate this paradox most effectively are those who have learned to separate their identity from any individual outcome. They can lose a round, lose a match, lose a training session, without interpreting that loss as a verdict on their worth as a person. The loss is data. The ego survives because it is attached to the long-term project, not the short-term result.

The Zone, Flow, and Why You Cannot Chase It

Sheridan engages seriously with the psychology of peak performance states — what athletes call being "in the zone" and what psychologists call flow. Multiple fighters describe moments of almost eerie clarity, where the fight seemed to slow down, decisions came without deliberation, and the body executed techniques automatically. Sheridan's argument, built from his interviews, is that these states cannot be directly produced by intention. You cannot decide to enter flow. What you can do is create the conditions under which flow becomes likely.

Those conditions include: sufficient mastery of technique that execution is automatic rather than deliberate; the right level of arousal — high enough for focus, low enough to avoid panic; a mindset oriented toward the process rather than the outcome; and enough psychological safety (created by thorough preparation) to release conscious control. The fighters who most reliably access these states are those who have drilled their techniques so deeply that their bodies can fight without consulting the conscious mind. The conscious mind, liberated from managing execution, becomes available for higher-level tactical reading of the opponent.

Confidence as Infrastructure, Not Attitude

A subtle but important distinction Sheridan draws is between confidence as a performed attitude and confidence as a structural property of the fighter's psychology. Fake confidence — the kind produced by positive self-talk and bravado — is brittle. It shatters on contact with real adversity because it has no foundation in genuine experience. Structural confidence is built from the accumulation of hard evidence: thousands of repetitions executed correctly, difficult opponents defeated in training, suffering endured and survived. This kind of confidence does not need to be performed because it is simply a factual summary of what the fighter knows about themselves.

Randy Couture exemplifies this model. He was repeatedly the underdog, repeatedly fighting younger and more physically imposing opponents, and he approached each of those fights with what appeared to observers as unusual calm. Sheridan's exploration suggests that calm was not suppression of fear but the product of genuine self-knowledge. Couture had done the work. He knew what he could do. The confidence was downstream of the preparation, not a substitute for it.

The Role of Narrative and Meaning

Sheridan argues that elite fighters are, among other things, exceptional narrators of their own experience. They have a story they tell themselves about who they are, why they fight, and what the struggle means. This narrative is not delusional or self-aggrandizing; it is a framework for organizing difficulty into something purposeful. A fighter who enters a difficult training camp with no story about why it matters will find reasons to quit. A fighter who has constructed a compelling narrative about their purpose — their responsibilities to family, their desire to prove something, their sense of identity as a competitor — has a renewable source of motivation that mere willpower cannot match.

This connects to Viktor Frankl's observation (referenced implicitly throughout the book) that humans can endure almost any suffering if they have a reason. The mental game in fighting is partly about engineering that reason. Coaches who understand this help their fighters build meaningful narratives around their training and competition, transforming grinding work into purposeful sacrifice.

Tactical Thinking Under Pressure: The Chess Model

Several fighters, particularly those with backgrounds in wrestling or jiu-jitsu, describe their mental experience of competition in explicitly tactical terms — as a form of physical chess. Josh Waitzkin, the chess prodigy turned martial artist, is particularly important here. His framework distinguishes between fighters who are reactive (responding to what the opponent does) and fighters who are creative (imposing their own structure on the fight). Reactive fighters are always slightly behind; creative fighters are always slightly ahead.

The mental skill that separates these two types is the ability to maintain a strategic overview while simultaneously executing at a technical level. This is enormously difficult because the cognitive demands of executing a technique under stress tend to consume all available attention. Fighters who can simultaneously manage the micro (this specific exchange) and the macro (the overall strategy of the match) are operating at a level of mental sophistication that is indistinguishable, from the outside, from physical dominance.

Learning to Lose and the Growth Mindset

Before Carol Dweck's framework became widely popularized, elite coaches in combat sports had already arrived at the same insight through practice. Sheridan documents the view, held by the best coaches he interviewed, that loss is an essential component of development and that fighters who are protected from losing — by coaches who match them only against beatable opponents — are being quietly sabotaged. Losing against a superior opponent, particularly in training, provides irreplaceable information about gaps in technique, conditioning, and mental resilience. Fighters who have learned to receive this information without ego collapse are developing at a faster rate than those who cannot.

3. Key Stories and Examples

Dan Gable's Obsession

Gable's story anchors Sheridan's argument about pain and preparation. Gable lost once in his collegiate wrestling career — a single match in his final NCAA tournament — and that loss haunted and motivated him for the rest of his life. As a coach at Iowa, he built a program famous for the sheer brutality of its training demands. His approach was not sadistic; it was philosophical. He believed that the wrestler who had suffered more in preparation would always defeat the one who had not, because suffering in competition would be a familiar territory to one and a shock to the other. His wrestlers were not merely better-conditioned; they were psychologically inoculated against the adversity a match could produce.

Marcelo Garcia's Openness

Garcia, arguably the greatest submission grappler in the history of Brazilian jiu-jitsu, provides a striking counter-narrative to the aggressive ego-driven fighter archetype. Sheridan describes Garcia as remarkably willing to be caught in bad positions in training, to experiment freely, and to accept submissions without frustration. Garcia's willingness to lose in practice created a learning environment of extraordinary richness. He was not protecting a record in the gym; he was building a map of every possible dangerous situation and finding his way out. By the time he competed, his familiarity with difficult positions meant that bad situations held no terror — he had already been there and found the exit.

Josh Waitzkin's Transfer

Waitzkin's journey from chess champion to martial arts competitor is Sheridan's primary case study in the transferability of mental skills. Waitzkin argues that the lessons he learned in chess — about managing pressure, building patterns, thinking several moves ahead, learning from defeat — translated almost directly into jiu-jitsu competition. What he had to acquire was the physical technique; the mental architecture was already in place. This story supports Sheridan's broader thesis that mental skill is domain-general in important ways. The specific content varies, but the underlying psychological capacities — focus, resilience, strategic thinking, ego management — are portable.

Randy Couture's Late-Career Excellence

Couture's ability to compete at elite level well past his physical prime is one of Sheridan's most compelling examples of mental advantage overcoming physical limitation. As Couture aged, his opponents were increasingly faster, stronger, and more explosive. Yet Couture continued to win. Sheridan attributes this to several factors: accumulated tactical intelligence that allowed Couture to anticipate and neutralize his opponents' advantages; psychological composure that prevented him from being rattled by early adversity; and a level of self-knowledge so precise that he could execute a game plan with extraordinary consistency. His fights were not won in the moments of athletic excellence but in the long hours of strategic preparation and the mental discipline to execute the plan under fire.

4. How the Argument Builds Across the Book

Sheridan structures the book not as a linear argument but as a series of immersive encounters, each illuminating a different dimension of mental toughness. Early chapters establish the foundational claims: that mental strength is trainable, that pain is partially a mental phenomenon, and that fear management is a learnable skill. These claims are grounded in the experiences of fighters and coaches who have encountered these realities at the most unforgiving level.

The middle sections deepen the argument by introducing complexity and paradox. The ego section complicates the simple story of mental toughness by showing that some forms of mental strength — rigidity, overconfidence, refusal to accept difficulty — are actually liabilities. Sheridan is careful not to present a one-dimensional picture of the ideal fighter's psychology. The mental game is nuanced, context-dependent, and full of tensions that cannot be resolved with simple formulas.

The later chapters move toward integration, bringing together figures like Waitzkin whose experiences allow Sheridan to articulate how these various mental skills relate to each other and how they can be developed systematically. The book closes not with a prescriptive training program but with a deepened appreciation for the complexity of peak mental performance — an appreciation that is itself a kind of intellectual humility, appropriate given the subject matter.

5. Honest Critiques and Limitations

The book's greatest strength — its immersive, character-driven journalism — is also its greatest limitation. Because Sheridan builds his argument through individual stories and interviews, the framework that emerges is rich but somewhat unsystematic. He does not construct a falsifiable model of mental performance or engage rigorously with the scientific literature on sports psychology, cognitive science, or psychophysiology. Readers looking for a structured, evidence-based framework will need to look elsewhere.

There is also a selection bias problem. Sheridan interviews champions and people who believe deeply in the mental dimension of performance. He does not seriously engage with the counterargument — that physical attributes, genetic endowment, and access to resources explain most of the variance in athletic outcomes. The mental game may be more important at the margin between two equally matched athletes than across the full distribution of competitors.

Additionally, the book's world is almost exclusively male. The mental game in women's combat sports, which has its own fascinating dimensions and has produced its own exceptional practitioners, is essentially invisible here. This is a significant gap.

Finally, some of Sheridan's examples, particularly around extreme training methods, could be read as inadvertently endorsing approaches that carry genuine injury risk. The line between productive suffering and self-destruction is not always clearly drawn.

6. How to Apply This

Personal Applications

Build genuine confidence through evidence, not affirmation. The most actionable lesson from the book is to invest in preparation so thorough that confidence becomes a logical inference. Before any high-stakes event — a presentation, a difficult conversation, a performance — accumulate evidence of your competence through deliberate practice. Stop trying to feel confident and start doing the things that make confidence earned.

Reframe your relationship with difficulty and discomfort. Train yourself to interpret physical and psychological discomfort as information rather than threat. In workouts, in cold conversations, in creative struggles, the discomfort is a signal that you are near the edge of your current capacity — which is exactly where growth happens. Practice staying present with difficulty rather than escaping it.

Separate your identity from individual outcomes. Develop the practice of evaluating your performance without merging it with your self-concept. Losses, failures, and rejections are data about what needs to improve, not verdicts about who you are. Keep a learning journal that treats setbacks as coaching opportunities.

Develop a narrative of purpose. Identify and articulate why the difficult thing you are pursuing matters to you at a deep level. This story will serve as motivation when willpower runs out, which it always does. The narrative does not need to be dramatic; it needs to be genuine and connected to your actual values.

Practice strategic thinking under pressure. Deliberately create conditions of mild stress during practice or preparation and train yourself to maintain a strategic overview. In negotiation, in project management, in creative work — practice zooming out to the strategic level even when the immediate pressure is intense.

Professional Applications

Coaching and leadership: The book's deepest lesson for leaders is that the goal is not to protect people from difficulty but to prepare them for it. Leaders who insulate their teams from hard challenges are creating fragility. Structure experiences of managed adversity that build genuine resilience.

Hiring and team development: Look for the capacity to learn from failure, not just the record of success. Candidates who have been tested, failed, and extracted lessons are more valuable than those with unblemished records achieved in protected environments.

Negotiation and high-stakes communication: The mental skills described — staying calm under pressure, maintaining strategic awareness, managing ego — are directly applicable to negotiation. Prepare so thoroughly that anxiety is replaced by genuine confidence. Develop the ability to lose a point in a negotiation without losing the thread of your strategy.

Five Key Takeaways

  1. Mental toughness is a skill, not a trait. It is built through deliberate practice, accumulated experience of difficulty, and the development of specific psychological capacities — not through genetics or force of will alone.

  2. Confidence is downstream of preparation. Genuine confidence is a factual summary of what you have done, not an attitude you perform. Build the evidence base and the confidence will follow.

  3. The ego must be large enough to drive ambition and flexible enough to allow learning. The inability to lose well — in training, in work, in life — is a developmental ceiling that no amount of talent can break through.

  4. Pain and suffering, reinterpreted as productive, become assets. The fighter who has voluntarily suffered more than the competition has converted an experience everyone tries to avoid into a genuine competitive advantage.

  5. Mental skills are domain-general. The capacities developed in any demanding discipline — focus, resilience, ego management, tactical thinking — transfer. The specific context is almost incidental; the underlying psychological infrastructure is what matters.

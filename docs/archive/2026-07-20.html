🏈 College Athletics (D1 Ticker Beat)

1. Protect College Sports Act Heads to a Critical Senate Floor Vote This Week

The Senate Commerce Committee advanced the Protect College Sports Act with a bipartisan 19–9 vote, sending it to the full Senate for consideration. A sponsor says momentum is real: Sen. Eric Schmitt declared the bill "probably" has 60 votes and called the next two weeks critical.

The Senate's summer recess runs from August 10 through September 11, leaving a narrow window for passage.

Senate Commerce Committee press release

2. Big Ten and SEC Remain the Key Holdouts — And Are Actively Negotiating

The Big Ten and SEC, the wealthiest conferences, oppose the bill as written; part of their objection centers on a provision that prevents conferences reporting over $1 billion in revenue from forming a "super-league."

Leaders from both conferences have met directly with top sponsors Cruz and Cantwell to push for changes. The outcome of those talks this week could determine whether the bill lives or dies before the August recess.

CBS Sports — Protect College Sports Act

3. Bill Would Cap Revenue Sharing at $21.3M and Arm-Wrestle the Transfer Portal

Without the legislation, conferences are limited to $21.3 million in revenue-sharing with players during the 2026–27 academic year under the House settlement terms.

Many of the nation's richest programs are already trying to circumvent that cap using business partners to supplement rosters with NIL contracts, pushing top football payrolls closer to $40 million.

The bill would cap agent fees at 5%, guarantee one transfer without penalty, and require a year of lost eligibility for a second transfer.

ESPN — Bipartisan college sports bill

4. NCAA President Baker Floats Regionalized Scheduling for Non-Football Sports

NCAA President Charlie Baker indicated that Division I administrators are actively discussing strategies to regionalize non-football scheduling to alleviate logistical burdens.

Baker appeared on national television to discuss the potential for basketball and other sports to reorganize based on geography. The conversation reflects growing anxiety that coast-to-coast travel costs are becoming unsustainable in a revenue-sharing era where every dollar counts.

Press Democrat — Baker/realignment mailbag

5. Big Ten's Unequal Revenue Distribution Model Seen as the Conference's Near-Term Future

In the era of revenue sharing and NIL, blue-blood programs are increasingly intent on keeping what they generate — and the immense media revenue collected by the Big Ten and SEC is effectively driven by just four or five schools per conference.

Unequal revenue distribution is already the model of the present in the ACC and is seen as the way of the future across Power conferences. Schools like Washington are reportedly uncertain they will receive full revenue shares once the Big Ten's next media cycle kicks off in 2030–31.

Press Democrat — Big Ten revenue splits

📊 Bloomberg

6. U.S. Conducts Ninth Straight Night of Iran Strikes; Gasoline Tops $4 a Gallon

The U.S. announced more attacks for a ninth consecutive night, with Iran continuing to respond by striking American allies across the Middle East.

U.S. pump prices climbed back above $4 a gallon as the conflict intensified — regular unleaded averaged $4.003 a gallon, threatening to stoke inflation. Iran has received proposals from mediators about resuming negotiations, though its foreign ministry said it would continue to defend itself.

Bloomberg — US Gasoline Tops $4

7. Global Oil Prices Jump as Hormuz Fears Resurge; Emerging Markets Rattled

Brent crude rose over 3% to above $90 a barrel and U.S. benchmark crude climbed nearly 3% as the conflict escalated.

Emerging-market stocks and currencies were mixed as investors weighed rising U.S.-Iran tensions on top of last week's technology-led global selloff.

Prices for road fuels in Europe are also soaring, nearing seasonal levels last seen in the aftermath of Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine.

Bloomberg — Emerging Markets/Iran Tensions

8. South Korea's Kospi Falls 4.5% as AI Stock Selloff Extends Into a New Week

Asian shares were mostly lower on Monday and South Korea's Kospi fell 4.5% as investors unloaded more stocks linked to artificial intelligence.

Samsung Electronics lost 4.3% and memory chipmaker SK Hynix fell 4.2%.

Pledges of massive AI spending are fueling worries the sector may be in a bubble, and many investors are selling to lock in profits from recent gains.

Bloomberg — War With Iran / Asia Trade

9. Paramount-Warner Bros. Merger Faces Court Ruling by Wednesday

A federal judge said she would rule by July 22 on a request by a dozen states to at least temporarily pause Paramount Skydance Corp.'s $110 billion takeover of Warner Bros. Discovery.

U.S. District Judge Araceli Martínez-Olguín in Oakland set the timetable after a hearing last Friday. The deal — which would unite studios behind Harry Potter, Mission: Impossible, CNN, and HBO — is one of the biggest media mergers in history and a ruling to pause it could throw the deal's timeline into chaos.

Bloomberg — Paramount-Warner Bros. ruling

10. CalPERS Launches Historic "Total Portfolio" Experiment — Now 3 Weeks In

CalPERS became the first U.S. public pension plan to embrace a total portfolio investment approach, as Bloomberg noted.

As of July 1, the new model replaced the old strategic asset allocation system, shifting the focus to which investments can best contribute to overall portfolio performance rather than hitting individual asset-class targets.

CalPERS' full portfolio returned a preliminary net 14.8% for the fiscal year ended June 30, outperforming its 6.8% assumed rate of return.

Bloomberg — CalPERS $600B Experiment

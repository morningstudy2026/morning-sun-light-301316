US senators recently introduced the bipartisan Protect College Sports Act of 2026, a sweeping proposal that would establish a federal framework for college athletics covering NIL compensation, athlete protections, and governance. The Illusion of Victory

by Thomas Fleming

The Illusion of Victory: A Detailed Key-Ideas Brief

Based on Thomas Fleming's historical analysis of America's entry into and conduct of World War I

1. Central Thesis and Why It Matters

Thomas Fleming's central argument is both provocative and sobering: America's participation in World War I was built on a foundation of manufactured consent, presidential deception, cynical power politics, and ideological overreach — and the "victory" that emerged from it was not a triumph of democratic values but a catastrophic failure that planted the seeds for everything that followed, including the rise of fascism, the Second World War, and decades of geopolitical instability.

The thesis operates on several interlocking levels. First, Fleming contends that Woodrow Wilson — far from being the noble idealist history has often portrayed — was a manipulative, morally self-righteous leader who bent truth, suppressed dissent, and ultimately betrayed the very principles he claimed to champion. Wilson's famous Fourteen Points and his crusade to "make the world safe for democracy" were, in Fleming's telling, less a genuine vision for international peace than a vehicle for Wilson's own messianic sense of mission and a mechanism for projecting American power onto a war-weary world.

Second, Fleming argues that the American public was not organically swept up in enthusiasm for war but was systematically propagandized into supporting it by the Committee on Public Information under George Creel, an apparatus that poisoned civic discourse, institutionalized intolerance, and set dangerous precedents for government manipulation of public opinion.

Third, the book asserts that the peace settlement at Versailles — which Wilson treated as the culmination of his crusade — was a humiliating reversal for Wilson's ideals, shaped instead by the vindictive priorities of France and Britain, and that Wilson's subsequent refusal to compromise with the Senate on the League of Nations guaranteed America would ratify nothing meaningful at all.

Why does this matter today? Because the pattern Fleming identifies — a president using war as an instrument of moral transformation, suppressing dissent in the name of patriotism, promising a world-changing peace while delivering something far uglier, and then refusing to accept political reality — is not a historical curiosity. It is a template that recurs. Fleming's book is implicitly a warning about the dangers of ideological warfare, the fragility of civil liberties during wartime, and the catastrophic consequences of confusing rhetoric with reality in foreign policy.

2. Major Frameworks, Models, and Arguments

The Wilsonian Messianism Framework

Fleming's most sustained analytical framework is what might be called Wilsonian Messianism — the idea that Wilson didn't merely pursue political objectives but genuinely believed he was divinely appointed to remake the world order. This wasn't standard political ambition dressed in religious language; Fleming argues it was a deep psychological and theological conviction rooted in Wilson's Presbyterian upbringing and his father's ministry.

The logic of this framework is important: when a leader believes he is doing God's work, ordinary political compromises become morally unacceptable. Negotiating with senators or adjusting positions in the face of criticism feels to such a leader like betraying a sacred mission. This helps explain why Wilson repeatedly chose ideological purity over political effectiveness — why he rejected Republican amendments to the League covenant that might have secured Senate ratification, why he refused to engage constructively with Henry Cabot Lodge, and why he launched a cross-country speaking tour while physically unwell rather than accept compromise. His stubbornness was not mere vanity; it was theological conviction made political.

This framework also explains the gap between Wilson's lofty rhetoric and his often brutal practice. He could speak of freedom while authorizing the Espionage Act and the Sedition Act. He could champion self-determination while ignoring the claims of colonized peoples at Paris. The messianic framework allowed him to believe that the ends — a transformed international order under his vision — justified whatever means were necessary.

The Manufactured Consent Model

Before Noam Chomsky made the phrase famous, Fleming's book documents in granular detail how consent for the war was engineered rather than organically formed. The Committee on Public Information (CPI), run by journalist George Creel, was essentially America's first modern propaganda ministry. Fleming frames this not as an unfortunate wartime necessity but as a corruption of democratic culture that had long-lasting effects.

The model works like this: In a democracy, a government cannot simply order its citizens to support a war — especially one that, as late as 1916, Wilson himself was campaigning against ("He kept us out of war"). It must persuade. But persuasion requires simplification, and simplification in wartime tends toward demonization. The CPI flooded American society with images of German barbarism, promoted vigilante suspicion of German-Americans, encouraged citizens to report "disloyal" neighbors, and turned dissent from a constitutional right into a quasi-criminal act.

Fleming traces the consequences: German-Americans — millions of whom had been loyal, productive citizens — were subjected to harassment, job loss, and sometimes violence. Socialists, pacifists, and labor leaders who opposed the war were imprisoned under the Espionage Act. The linguistic and cultural richness of German-American life was scrubbed from public view almost overnight. Sauerkraut was renamed "liberty cabbage." Cities with German names were renamed. This cultural erasure, Fleming suggests, was a harbinger of the kind of enforced conformity that would characterize twentieth-century authoritarian states.

The deeper argument is that these propaganda mechanisms, once created, don't simply disappear after the war ends. They establish templates, precedents, and institutional habits that future governments can adapt.

The Allied Deception Framework

Fleming builds a compelling case that Britain and France were not transparent partners in a democratic crusade but shrewd manipulators who used Wilson's idealism to extract American blood and treasure while pursuing their own entirely different war aims. The British in particular had perfected the art of shaping American opinion — through control of transatlantic cable communications, through cultivated relationships with American journalists and intellectuals, and through strategic management of the information flow from Europe.

The framework here is essentially one of asymmetric information: the British government understood far better than Wilson what the war was actually about (imperial competition, revenge, territorial acquisition) and what the peace settlement would actually produce. They let Wilson's idealist rhetoric do the work of mobilizing American opinion while privately dismissing his Fourteen Points as naive. When it came time to negotiate at Versailles, the British and French had no intention of honoring the spirit of those points.

Fleming uses this framework to explain what he sees as Wilson's tragic naivety at Paris. Wilson arrived believing his enormous prestige would allow him to impose his vision on the settlement. He was instead slowly outmaneuvered by Clemenceau and Lloyd George, who understood that Wilson needed a treaty — any treaty — to justify the war's costs, and who exploited that need to extract punitive terms against Germany that virtually guaranteed future conflict.

The Senate as Constitutional Safeguard

One of Fleming's more counterintuitive arguments is a rehabilitation of Henry Cabot Lodge and the Republican Senate opposition. In conventional histories, Lodge is often portrayed as a small-minded partisan who sabotaged Wilson's visionary peace plan. Fleming turns this narrative around: Lodge was doing exactly what senators are constitutionally supposed to do — scrutinizing a treaty that would commit America to international obligations without clear limits, without Senate oversight, and without any guaranteed mechanism for exit.

The framework here is constitutional fidelity versus executive overreach. Fleming argues that Article X of the League Covenant — which would have committed the United States to defending any League member against aggression — was a genuine constitutional problem, not merely a partisan objection. It effectively transferred the war-making power from Congress to an international body, bypassing the constitutional requirement for Congressional declarations of war.

Lodge's reservations, in this reading, were reasonable amendments that Wilson could have accepted at relatively low cost to the substance of his vision. Wilson refused not because the reservations were substantively devastating but because accepting them would have meant admitting that other people had valid points — something his messianic self-image could not accommodate.

The Domestic Tyranny Paradox

Perhaps the most troubling framework in the book is what might be called the Domestic Tyranny Paradox: a war fought in the name of democracy at home produced the most serious suppression of civil liberties in American history up to that point. Fleming documents how the Espionage Act of 1917 and the Sedition Act of 1918 effectively criminalized opposition to the war. People were imprisoned for writing anti-war pamphlets, for criticizing the draft, for speaking German in public. Socialist leader Eugene Debs was sentenced to ten years in prison for a speech opposing the war.

The paradox is not incidental — it is structural. When a war is justified in explicitly moral terms, as a crusade for universal values, then opposition to the war becomes not merely political disagreement but moral treason. If we are fighting for democracy itself, what kind of person opposes our fight? A traitor. A coward. An enemy. The moral framing of the war inevitably produces the moral demonization of dissenters.

3. Most Important Supporting Examples and Stories

The Lusitania and the Question of Provocation

Fleming revisits the sinking of the Lusitania with careful attention to what was known and suppressed at the time. The German government had placed advertisements in American newspapers warning passengers that the ship was entering a war zone and that it was fair game. What the American public was not told — and what Wilson's administration had reason to know — was that the Lusitania was carrying munitions in addition to passengers, making it a legitimate military target under international law. The emotional fury generated by the sinking was real, but it was constructed on selective information. Fleming uses this episode to establish early in the book that the path to American intervention was paved with omissions as much as with outright lies.

George Creel and the Four-Minute Men

One of Fleming's most vivid illustrations of wartime propaganda is his account of the "Four-Minute Men" — an army of approximately seventy-five thousand volunteer speakers organized by Creel's CPI who would appear in movie theaters, churches, and public gathering places to deliver exactly four-minute speeches promoting the war. These were not spontaneous expressions of civic enthusiasm; they were centrally coordinated, script-approved, and systematically deployed. Fleming uses this detail to make the propaganda apparatus concrete and human-scaled — not some faceless bureaucratic machine but thousands of ordinary Americans enlisted as instruments of government persuasion.

The Treatment of Eugene Debs

Debs was one of the most beloved political figures in early twentieth-century America, a man who had received nearly a million votes for president as a socialist candidate. When he delivered a speech in Canton, Ohio, in 1918 expressing sympathy for those imprisoned for opposing the war, he was arrested and sentenced to ten years in prison. Wilson personally refused to pardon him, even after the armistice. Fleming uses Debs's story to put a human face on the domestic tyranny he documents and to illustrate Wilson's particular vindictiveness toward those who challenged his moral authority.

Wilson at Paris

Fleming's portrait of Wilson at the Paris Peace Conference is almost operatic in its tragedy. Wilson arrived in Europe to extraordinary popular acclaim — crowds in Paris, Rome, and London treated him as a near-messianic figure, a liberator. He was greeted as no previous American president had ever been greeted abroad. But this popular adulation masked his diplomatic weakness: Clemenceau and Lloyd George had concrete goals and the cynical flexibility to pursue them. Wilson had principles and the rigid conviction that they would prevail.

Fleming traces the successive compromises Wilson made — allowing the humiliating "war guilt" clause, accepting reparations far beyond anything Germany could reasonably pay, permitting colonial dispositions that violated the principle of self-determination when it inconvenienced the Allies — as the step-by-step demolition of his own program. By the end of the conference, Wilson had retained the League of Nations (his non-negotiable priority) but had sacrificed virtually everything else. And then the Senate refused even that.

The Collapse of Wilson's Health

Wilson's cross-country speaking tour to drum up public support for the League treaty is one of the most poignant and revealing episodes in the book. Against the advice of his doctor and his wife, Wilson traveled thousands of miles, delivering speech after speech, visibly deteriorating. He suffered a major stroke that effectively incapacitated him for the remainder of his presidency. Fleming uses this episode not just as personal drama but as a window into Wilson's psychology: he would rather destroy himself than adapt. The country was effectively leaderless for months during a critical period of postwar adjustment, with Wilson's wife Edith managing access to the president and shielding the extent of his incapacity from the public and even from Congress.

4. How the Argument Builds Across the Book

Fleming structures his argument as a progressive revelation of disillusionment. The book opens with the pre-war period and Wilson's genuine initial desire to keep America out of the conflict, establishing the gap between Wilson's rhetoric and his eventual actions. The early chapters build the case for manufactured consent and propaganda, documenting the infrastructure of public persuasion that was assembled even before formal entry into the war.

The middle sections follow the American experience of the war itself — the military contribution, which was real and significant in the war's final phase, but also the home-front repression, the persecution of German-Americans, and the suppression of dissent. Fleming is careful to acknowledge American military valor without letting it obscure the political corruption that surrounded it.

The final sections build toward the double failure at Paris and in the Senate. By the time Fleming reaches these chapters, he has established all the elements needed to explain both failures: Wilson's messianic rigidity, Allied cynicism, the constitutional legitimacy of Senate concerns, and the catastrophic mismatch between Wilson's promises and the peace he actually delivered. The book ends not with the armistice but with the wreckage — a peace settlement that punished Germany so harshly it made the next war nearly inevitable, and an American public left with a nagging sense that something had gone profoundly wrong.

5. Honest Critiques and Limitations

Prosecutorial rather than analytical tone. Fleming is a gifted narrative historian, but his portrait of Wilson sometimes reads more like an indictment than a balanced assessment. Wilson's genuine achievements — including his role in eventually establishing the framework for international institutions — receive less generous treatment than his failures. Readers looking for a full accounting of Wilson's presidency will need to supplement this book with more sympathetic treatments.

Selective contextualization. The British and French leaders come across as almost cartoonishly cynical in contrast to Wilson's naive idealism. In reality, Clemenceau was responding to genuine French security needs after being invaded twice in fifty years, and his "vindictiveness" was comprehensible within that context. Fleming does not give sufficient weight to the structural constraints these leaders faced.

Limited attention to German responsibility. In building the case that the peace settlement was unjust, Fleming sometimes soft-pedals the genuine German role in initiating and escalating the war. The "war guilt" clause was legally absurd, but Germany did bear substantial responsibility for the conflict's outbreak. The book's argument would be strengthened by engaging more directly with this complexity.

Anachronistic expectations. Some of Fleming's criticism of Wilson for failing to champion the rights of colonized peoples imposes expectations that were far outside the mainstream political imagination of 1919. This doesn't excuse Wilson's racial blindness, but it complicates the moral accounting.

American-centric frame. The book is primarily concerned with what the war did to America and to American democratic culture. The experience of Europeans — for whom the war was an unimaginably greater catastrophe — remains largely in the background. This framing is understandable but limits the book's scope.

6. How to Apply This

Personal Applications

Recognize manufactured consensus. When you find yourself surrounded by seemingly unanimous public opinion on a contentious issue, apply Fleming's skepticism. Ask: Who benefits from this consensus? What mechanisms produced it? What dissenting voices are being marginalized or silenced? The history Fleming documents is a reminder that democratic societies are not immune to coordinated campaigns that masquerade as organic public opinion.

Beware of messianic self-certainty. Wilson's tragedy is a parable about the dangers of absolute conviction in one's own moral rightness. In personal and professional life, the person who is most certain they are doing God's work is often the least able to hear valid criticism, learn from mistakes, or adapt to reality. Cultivate the discipline of engaging seriously with those who disagree with you, especially when their disagreement is inconvenient.

Separate rhetoric from reality in the people you follow. Wilson is a masterclass in the gap between soaring ideals and actual practice. When evaluating leaders — political, organizational, intellectual — apply the same scrutiny to their practice as to their words. What do they actually do when their stated values conflict with their interests?

Professional Applications

Understand the propaganda mechanics in your industry. Every industry has its version of Creel's CPI — mechanisms that manage opinion, shape narratives, and marginalize inconvenient truths. Understanding these mechanisms, whether in media, marketing, finance, or politics, is a prerequisite for professional judgment that isn't merely a reflection of dominant narratives.

Apply constitutional-style skepticism to irreversible commitments. Lodge's critique of the League was partly about the danger of open-ended commitments without exit provisions. In business, organizational, and personal contexts, be wary of agreements that lock you into undefined future obligations. The value of any commitment is partly a function of the clarity of its terms.

Recognize the cost of refusing to compromise. Wilson's refusal to accept Lodge's reservations is one of the most consequential cases of ideological inflexibility in political history. It resulted in getting nothing instead of most of what he wanted. In any negotiation — whether over a contract, a policy, or a relationship — the perfect should not be the enemy of the achievable.

Five Key Takeaways

  1. Wars justified on moral grounds tend to produce domestic tyranny. When a conflict is framed as a fight between absolute good and absolute evil, dissent becomes treason. The suppression of civil liberties in World War I America was not an accident but a logical consequence of the war's ideological framing — a lesson that applies to any crusade, political or otherwise.

  2. Messianic leadership is a danger, not a virtue. Wilson's certainty that he was divinely appointed to remake the world was not a source of strength but the root cause of his catastrophic failures. Leaders who cannot hear criticism, cannot accept compromise, and cannot distinguish their personal convictions from objective truth tend to cause the most damage precisely when their goals are most ambitious.

  3. Propaganda infrastructure, once built, outlasts the emergency that created it. The Committee on Public Information was dissolved after the war, but the techniques, precedents, and cultural habits it created did not disappear. Every democracy should be deeply suspicious of government-managed opinion-shaping, even — especially — in wartime.

  4. The gap between promised outcomes and actual results is where history's most dangerous resentments are born. The Versailles settlement wasn't just unjust; it was a betrayal of specific promises that millions of people had been told justified enormous sacrifice. Resentment born from that gap helped fuel the catastrophes of the following decades. This principle applies at every scale: organizations and leaders who overpromise and underdeliver are not merely embarrassed — they create conditions for backlash.

  5. Understanding how a war ends matters as much as understanding why it starts. American strategic thinking in 1917 focused almost entirely on winning the war and very little on what a just and stable peace would require. The result was a military victory that produced a political catastrophe. Any serious engagement with conflict — at the national, organizational, or personal level — must think through the endgame with the same rigor applied to the opening moves.

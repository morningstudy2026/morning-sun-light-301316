1. NCAA's New Age-Based Eligibility Rule Faces Class-Action Antitrust Challenge

A proposed class action lawsuit alleges that the NCAA's newly adopted age-based eligibility rule unlawfully restricts competition by limiting many college athletes' opportunities to compete, receive athletic scholarships, and earn compensation through NIL deals and direct revenue-sharing payments.

The rule, adopted on June 23, 2026, replaced the organization's prior eligibility framework for most athletes with an age-based system that bars otherwise eligible athletes from competing solely because of their age.

Under the new framework, Division I athletes generally receive five years of eligibility without traditional redshirts, with the clock beginning at initial full-time collegiate enrollment or the year following their 19th birthday — a shift that may have significant implications for recruiting, the transfer portal, and revenue-sharing decisions.

2. Protect College Sports Act Advances — But SEC and Big Ten Hold Back

On May 27, 2026, Senators Cruz and Cantwell announced the Protect College Sports Act, a sweeping bipartisan bill that would grant the NCAA and the College Sports Commission a limited antitrust exemption, enabling them to enforce rules around eligibility, transfers, and compensation without constant threat of state court litigation or competing state NIL regimes.

The bill codifies key elements of the House settlement into federal law and empowers the CSC to police alleged above-cap spending, including redirecting corporate sponsorship dollars to rosters as third-party NIL to circumvent the $21.3 million annual cap.

Notably, the SEC and Big Ten commissioners were absent from an endorsement letter signed by ACC and Big 12 leadership, and SEC Commissioner Greg Sankey publicly questioned the process — though the bipartisan architecture gives the bill a realistic chance of advancing further than its predecessors.

Some FBS schools are instructing the NCAA not to share their annual athletic financial information with the College Sports Commission — the enforcement entity created under the House settlement — in what has emerged as a flashpoint and a fresh sign of the CSC's authority being challenged.

The cap recalculation formula is one reason the CSC would want the data — but that rationale doesn't explain why non-power-conference schools like Miami are also being asked to share their financials.

II · Today's Book

Unreasonable Hospitality

by Will Guidara

Unreasonable Hospitality by Will Guidara — Key-Ideas Brief

1. Central Thesis and Why It Matters

Will Guidara's core argument is disarmingly simple but radically demanding: in any field, in any industry, at any scale, the deliberate decision to make people feel genuinely, deeply, and sometimes extravagantly cared for is not a soft or peripheral strategy — it is the highest-leverage competitive advantage available to a human being or an organization. The word "unreasonable" in the title is doing serious work. Guidara is not advocating for politeness or good service or even excellence. He is advocating for a posture toward people that most organizations would classify as economically irrational, operationally excessive, or simply unnecessary. His entire career at Eleven Madison Park (EMP) — the New York fine-dining restaurant that climbed from a middling, directionless space to the number-one restaurant in the world — becomes the laboratory for testing this thesis under real conditions.

Why does this matter beyond restaurants? Guidara makes the case that the hospitality industry has always been a mirror for the human condition at large. Every business is, at its core, in the business of making people feel something. The product or service is the vehicle; the feeling is the destination. As markets commoditize and artificial intelligence erases transactional advantages, the one thing that becomes more valuable is a felt sense of being seen, valued, and cared for. Unreasonable hospitality, then, is not a luxury for high-end establishments — it is a survival skill and a differentiator for anyone who wants their work to matter to the people they serve.

The thesis also contains an implicit critique of the modern business world's obsession with efficiency and scalability. Guidara argues that the chase for efficiency has systematically stripped human warmth out of service, and that the organizations willing to deliberately reintroduce it — even at apparent cost — will find that the returns, both emotional and financial, dwarf the investment.

2. Major Frameworks, Models, and Arguments

The 95/5 Rule

One of Guidara's most practically actionable frameworks is the 95/5 principle, which he developed with chef Daniel Humm. The idea is that an organization should be ruthlessly disciplined about controlling costs across 95 percent of its operations so that it can spend freely and generously on the remaining 5 percent that creates magic. The logic is not that the 5 percent is trivial — quite the opposite. That 5 percent is the only part of the experience that people will actually remember and talk about. The mistake most businesses make is either spending the 5 percent on the wrong things (visible but meaningless upgrades) or not reserving it at all, grinding every dollar into operational efficiency until the experience becomes sterile. The 95 percent discipline funds the 5 percent delight, and the 5 percent delight justifies charging for everything the 95 percent produces. The framework requires extraordinary operational rigor in service of extraordinary human moments.

The Four Pillars of Hospitality at EMP

Guidara organizes his philosophy around four interdependent pillars:

1. Dream, Obsess, Create: Great hospitality begins with an almost irrational imaginative commitment — asking not "what does a good restaurant do?" but "what is the most extraordinary version of this experience anyone has ever imagined?" EMP's team obsessed over every element of the guest journey, treating each dinner as a theatrical production with no small details. This pillar insists that imagination is a professional skill, not a personality trait.

2. Learn, Grow, Master: Guidara is emphatic that technical excellence is the necessary foundation on which hospitality rests. You cannot charm your way past incompetence. The team at EMP was expected to achieve genuine mastery — of wine, of food, of service choreography — because only mastery creates the headroom to be generous. Amateurs are too anxious about execution to notice the people in front of them.

3. Lead with Genuine Love: This sounds sentimental, but Guidara means something precise by it. True hospitality requires actually caring about the people you serve — not performing care as a technique, but developing a genuine curiosity about who they are and what would make this particular moment meaningful for them specifically. He argues that this love is trainable, not innate, but it requires an organizational culture that models it from the top down.

4. Make It Count: Hospitality without urgency becomes complacency. Every meal is irreversible. Every missed opportunity to connect with a guest is gone forever. Guidara cultivates a culture of urgency — not anxiety, but the deep awareness that this moment, this person, this dinner, is happening once and deserves the best effort available.

The 51/49 Rule of Talent

Guidara introduces a counterintuitive hiring and promotion framework: when evaluating candidates, give 51 percent of your weight to hospitality instinct (the natural desire to make others feel good) and only 49 percent to technical skill. This doesn't mean skills don't matter — they matter enormously — but technical skills can be trained more reliably than character. Someone with deep hospitality instinct but middling technique can be elevated; someone with extraordinary technique but zero warmth will cap out and potentially poison the culture. The ratio is deliberately narrow to prevent the principle from being used as an excuse to hire incompetent people — the 49 percent is still nearly half. But in a tie, heart wins.

Constant, Gentle Pressure

Guidara describes his management philosophy as "constant, gentle pressure" — a phrase that encapsulates his approach to leadership. He is relentless about standards, not in a tyrannical way but in the way of someone who truly believes in what the work can be and refuses to let it settle for less. He pushes his team continuously, but with a warmth that makes the push feel like an invitation to grow rather than a judgment. This requires leaders to have enough emotional intelligence to read when pressure is motivating versus crushing, and to calibrate accordingly. The "gentle" is not softness — it is precision. It is the difference between a coach and a bully.

Hospitality as a Verb, Not a Noun

One of Guidara's most important conceptual arguments is the distinction between hospitality as a department or a mood and hospitality as a continuous active practice. Most organizations treat hospitality as a noun — a service style, a value statement on the wall, a training module. Guidara insists it must be a verb — something done intentionally in each specific interaction, customized to the specific person, moment, and need. This makes hospitality inherently non-scalable in the conventional sense (you cannot automate genuine attention to individuals), which is exactly why it is so defensible as a competitive moat.

The Gift Economy of Experience

Guidara borrows implicitly from anthropological ideas about gift exchange to argue that when you give someone an unexpected, unreasonable gift of attention or experience — something they could not have anticipated and did not ask for — you create a bond that transactional exchange cannot replicate. The surprise element is crucial. A gift that is expected becomes a baseline; it loses its emotional charge. Unreasonable hospitality works precisely because it exceeds expectations dramatically and without warning. The emotional residue of such moments lasts far longer than the moment itself.

3. Important Stories and Examples

The Hot Dog Story

Perhaps the most quoted anecdote in the book illustrates the entire philosophy in miniature. A table of European guests at EMP was overheard by the service team mentioning that they had not yet had a New York City hot dog during their visit and were disappointed they probably wouldn't get to. The team could have smiled and moved on. Instead, someone sprinted to a nearby hot dog cart, bought hot dogs, and the kitchen prepared a perfectly plated, elegantly presented version of street food to deliver to the table as a surprise mid-meal course. The cost was almost nothing. The time investment was minimal. But the guests — eating at one of the best restaurants in the world — were completely undone by the fact that someone had actually listened and then acted on what they heard. This story encapsulates the 95/5 rule, hospitality as a verb, and the gift economy all at once.

The Road to Number One

Guidara takes over the management of EMP when it is already technically accomplished but soulless — a restaurant that delivers technically correct food in a technically correct environment but makes no one feel anything. The journey to the number-one ranking in the World's 50 Best list is not primarily a story about cooking (Daniel Humm handles that side) — it is a story about the deliberate reimagining of the service experience as an emotional art form equal in ambition to the culinary art. Guidara describes this evolution through specific service innovations: walking guests through the kitchen, personalizing menus with guests' names, researching regulars so deeply that the team could surprise them with references to their histories. Every one of these changes was resisted initially on grounds of cost, practicality, or precedent — and every one of them proved to pay dividends in loyalty and reputation.

Learning to Cry (and Why That Matters)

Guidara recounts an early experience where he was moved to tears witnessing a perfectly executed moment of hospitality — a guest receiving an unexpected gesture at a moment of personal significance. He initially felt embarrassed by his own emotional response. Over time, he reframed this: the ability to be moved by beautiful human moments is not a weakness in a hospitality leader — it is a sensor. Leaders who are still capable of being genuinely affected by the emotional power of their work can recognize and celebrate those moments in their teams. Leaders who have become numb to it can no longer lead a culture of care.

The Christmas Story

Guidara describes a moment late in EMP's evolution when a family dining with them mentioned, almost in passing, that they were spending Christmas in New York and were slightly melancholy about missing their home traditions. The team quietly, without announcement, transformed a corner of the restaurant into something resembling a Christmas morning scene — small wrapped gifts, decorations, a sense of magic — as a surprise for the family when they returned to their table. The gesture was entirely unreasonable by any business logic. It was also entirely unforgettable, for the guests and for the team that pulled it off. Guidara uses this story to argue that unreasonable hospitality elevates the people who give it as much as those who receive it.

4. How the Argument Builds Across the Book

Guidara structures his argument progressively. The early chapters establish the context: a young, ambitious restaurateur inheriting a space with potential but no soul, learning the basics of hospitality from mentors (most notably Danny Meyer, whose book Setting the Table is a clear spiritual predecessor). This foundation-building phase is about learning that technical competence is necessary but insufficient.

The middle sections chart the active construction of EMP's hospitality philosophy — the experiments, failures, and breakthroughs that shaped the 95/5 model, the 51/49 hiring framework, and the culture of creative service. These chapters are the richest in practical frameworks and stories.

The latter sections zoom out to examine what happened once EMP reached the pinnacle — and crucially, what that success cost. Guidara is candid about the personal and organizational toll of obsessive pursuit of excellence. The book does not sanitize the burnout, the strained relationships, and the eventual separation from Daniel Humm and EMP. These chapters serve as a genuine reckoning with the shadow side of the thesis: unreasonable commitment to anything has consequences that must be consciously managed.

The final arc turns toward legacy and application — asking how the principles developed in the pressure cooker of a three-Michelin-star restaurant can be applied by anyone, in any context, who wants to make their work feel like a gift to the people it touches.

5. Honest Critiques and Limitations

The survivorship bias problem: EMP is an extreme environment — a restaurant with a fixed number of covers per night, a sky-high price point, and the financial capacity to invest in extraordinary gestures. The 95/5 model works beautifully when the 95 percent generates enough revenue to fund meaningful 5 percent moments. For a small business operating on razor-thin margins, the arithmetic looks different. Guidara nods at this tension but doesn't fully resolve it.

The personal cost is underexamined: While Guidara does address burnout and the end of his EMP partnership with honesty, the book stops short of a rigorous examination of what sustained "unreasonable" commitment does to people over time. For readers in high-pressure environments looking for permission to demand more of themselves and their teams, the book could more carefully articulate the conditions under which intensity becomes harm.

Scalability skepticism: The most powerful moments of hospitality described in the book are inherently artisanal — requiring human attention, memory, and improvisation that resist systematization. Guidara's principles are more portable than they might initially appear, but any organization trying to operationalize them at scale will face genuine challenges that the book doesn't fully address.

The celebrity-establishment advantage: EMP's ability to attract and develop world-class talent is partially a function of its prestige and location. Not every aspiring hospitality leader can recruit from the same pool or set the same table.

Emotional intelligence as assumed, not developed: Guidara advocates powerfully for leading with love and warmth, but the book doesn't provide a robust developmental curriculum for leaders who weren't raised with these instincts. The argument that these qualities can be trained is asserted more than demonstrated.

6. How to Apply This

Personal Applications

Audit your 5 percent. Think about the last ten meaningful interactions you had with friends, colleagues, or family. How many of them were technically correct but emotionally empty? Begin identifying the moments where a small, unexpected gesture — a specific compliment, an unexpected follow-through on something someone mentioned in passing, a handwritten note — could transform a routine exchange into something memorable.

Practice noticing. Guidara's most powerful skill is attention — the ability to hear what people are actually saying beneath what they're saying. Start actively listening in conversations for unexpressed desires or unmet needs. Then, when possible, act on what you hear.

Train your hospitality instinct. Take one relationship in your life — professional or personal — and spend two weeks deliberately thinking about what would make that person feel genuinely seen and valued. Then do it. This is a muscle; it grows with use.

Build your own 95/5 budget. Whether time, money, or energy, identify the resources you burn on maintenance and baseline execution, and deliberately protect a small portion for unreasonable generosity. Even 30 minutes a week reserved for one genuine act of unexpected care changes the texture of relationships over time.

Professional Applications

Redesign your onboarding or intake experience around the question: "What would make this person feel like they've arrived somewhere that was expecting them and is genuinely glad they're here?" Most onboarding is logistical. Make it emotional.

Apply the 51/49 rule to your next hire. After you've confirmed baseline technical competence, weight your final decision heavily toward the person who makes other people feel good. Culture is built from the accumulation of individual temperaments.

Implement constant, gentle pressure. Identify one standard in your team or organization that has slipped because no one wants to be the person who raises it. Raise it — not as a criticism but as a genuine expression of belief in what the work could be.

Create a "magic" budget. Designate a small pool of discretionary resources specifically reserved for unexpected generosity toward clients, guests, or team members. Remove the bureaucratic friction from using it. The existence of the budget signals that the organization takes unreasonable hospitality seriously.

Debrief for delight, not just defect. Most post-mortems focus on what went wrong. Add a standing question to your team meetings: "What was the most memorable thing we did for someone this week, and how do we do more of that?"

Five Key Takeaways

  1. Unreasonable hospitality is a competitive strategy, not a sentiment. In a world where products and services converge toward parity, how people feel in your presence is your most defensible differentiation.

  2. The 95/5 rule is the operating engine. Ruthless efficiency in the majority of your operations is what funds the generous, surprising, memorable gestures in the minority — and those gestures are what people actually remember.

  3. Hospitality is attention made visible. The technical move is to listen carefully enough to know what would genuinely move someone, and then to act on that knowledge without being asked. This is trainable, not innate.

  4. Excellence is the prerequisite, not the goal. Technical mastery creates the headroom to be generous. Without it, you're too anxious about execution to notice the person in front of you. Master the craft first; then go further.

  5. The unreasonable act elevates the giver as much as the receiver. Teams that regularly perform acts of extraordinary care develop a sense of meaning and identity that no compensation package can replicate. Unreasonable hospitality is how work becomes worth doing.

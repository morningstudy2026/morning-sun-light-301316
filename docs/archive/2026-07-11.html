1. Protect College Sports Act Awaits Full Senate Vote in a Narrow July Window

The bipartisan Protect College Sports Act cleared the Senate Commerce Committee 19–9 in June, marking the first time a college sports reform bill has advanced this far in the Senate and setting up a potential floor vote before the August recess.

Senate Commerce Chairman Ted Cruz told reporters that Majority Leader John Thune "intends" to bring the bill to the Senate floor, and Cruz believes that will happen in July.

The Senate's scheduled summer recess runs from Aug. 10 through Sept. 11, leaving a narrow window to reach the 60 votes needed for passage.

2. NIL Clearinghouse Has Rejected Nearly $90 Million in Deals in Its First Year

The College Sports Commission's clearinghouse has rejected nearly $90 million in NIL deals since its inception a year ago; but NIL Go, operated by Deloitte, has also approved $355 million in agreements since it launched in June 2025.

During the 61-day period from May 1 through June 30, the CSC rejected NIL deals worth $34 million and cleared deals worth $113 million; the average cleared deal was worth $14,792, while the average rejected deal was valued at $51,593.

Schools including Kentucky, Ohio State, and Utah have reorganized their athletic departments or created separate entities to facilitate NIL opportunities, and these have faced the most scrutiny as schools search for loopholes to exceed the $20.5 million revenue-sharing cap.

3. Revenue-Sharing Cap Grows to $21.3M at the One-Year Mark of the House Settlement

July 1 marked the one-year anniversary of the revenue-sharing era in college athletics, and the annual cap schools can directly share with athletes grew to $21.3 million.

Schools are distributing the vast majority of that money to football and men's basketball programs, as they drive much of the revenue for athletic department budgets — Texas Tech, for example, has allocated 74% to football, 17–18% to men's basketball, and just 2% to women's basketball.

This could lead to claims under Title IX, and female athletes have challenged the lawsuit settlement that created the direct compensation system, with a federal appeals court yet to rule on their case.

4. New Age-Based Eligibility Model Reshapes Roster Planning at Every Program

In June 2026, the NCAA adopted a new age-based eligibility model that will significantly alter roster management; under the new framework, Division I athletes generally receive five years of eligibility without traditional redshirts, and the eligibility clock begins at initial full-time collegiate enrollment or the academic year following an athlete's nineteenth birthday, whichever occurs first.

The rule may have significant implications for recruiting, transfer portal activity, roster construction, and revenue-sharing decisions, as institutions may increasingly evaluate athlete compensation through the lens of a five-year participation window.

II · Today's Book

The Power of Full Engagement

by Jim Loehr, Tony Schwartz

The Power of Full Engagement: A Detailed Key-Ideas Brief

1. Central Thesis and Why It Matters

Most people think the fundamental problem with performance and productivity is a shortage of time. We chase time-management systems, squeeze more tasks into fewer hours, and measure our effectiveness by how busy we are. Jim Loehr and Tony Schwartz argue this diagnosis is fundamentally wrong — and that getting the diagnosis wrong means all the solutions we try are also wrong.

The real scarce resource is not time. It is energy.

Time is fixed and non-renewable; a lost hour is gone forever. But energy is dynamic, renewable, and manageable. The central argument of this book is that peak performance — in work, relationships, health, and meaning — is a product of skillfully managing four distinct dimensions of personal energy: physical, emotional, mental, and spiritual. When all four are appropriately fueled, rhythmically expended, and deliberately recovered, a person can bring their full self to whatever they do. That state of optimal engagement is called Full Engagement.

The reason this thesis matters is both practical and philosophical. On the practical side, the research the authors bring from elite sports performance consulting — particularly from the Human Performance Institute where Loehr worked with world-class athletes — demonstrates that managing energy, not time, is what separates people who sustain excellence from those who burn out or underperform. On the philosophical side, the book asks a deeper question: what is the point of a life that is technically efficient but fundamentally disengaged? A person who manages their calendar perfectly but drains their passion, health, and connection has not solved the problem of living well.

Why now? Because the demands of modern professional life have made the problem acute in ways previous generations didn't face. Knowledge workers are expected to be "on" nearly all the time, the boundaries between work and recovery have dissolved, and the cultural celebration of busyness actively discourages the rest and renewal that the human system requires. The result is a workforce that is chronically underperforming not because of laziness, but because of mismanaged energy.

2. Major Frameworks, Models, and Arguments

The Four Dimensions of Energy

The backbone of the entire book is the argument that human beings are complex energy systems operating across four interconnected layers:

Physical Energy is the foundation. Without it, none of the other layers function well. Physical energy derives from sleep, nutrition, movement, and rhythmic stress-and-recovery cycles. The body is the instrument through which everything else is expressed, and neglecting it creates a cascading deficit across emotional, mental, and spiritual energy. This is why a person who is sleep-deprived makes worse ethical choices, processes information more slowly, and loses emotional resilience — physical insufficiency bleeds upward.

Emotional Energy is the quality of the energy the body generates. Even if a person has high physical energy, if they are chronically anxious, angry, or emotionally depleted, performance suffers dramatically. The authors argue that positive emotions — particularly confidence, challenge-seeking, connection, and joy — don't just feel good; they are measurably performance-enhancing because they expand perception, improve problem-solving, and sustain motivation. Emotional energy is managed by developing emotional self-awareness and deliberately cultivating positive emotional states.

Mental Energy is the capacity for focused attention, realistic optimism, and cognitive flexibility. Clear thinking, planning, and creative problem-solving all draw on mental energy. The key insight here is that mental energy is also renewable and is damaged by the same ignoring of oscillation that damages physical energy. Sustained mental effort without recovery leads to poor judgment, rigidity, and reduced creativity.

Spiritual Energy is perhaps the most unconventional inclusion. The authors use the word "spiritual" in a deliberately non-religious sense. It refers to the energy derived from having a clear sense of purpose, living in alignment with one's deepest values, and feeling that one's work and life are meaningful. Spiritual energy is the most powerful motivator and the one that most directly determines whether people sustain effort over the long run. People who know why they are doing what they are doing can tolerate almost any how.

These four dimensions are hierarchical in that physical is the foundation, but they are also mutually influencing. A purpose-driven motivation (spiritual) can propel a person through physical discomfort. Emotional instability can drain mental energy. The four dimensions must be managed together as a system.

The Performance Pyramid

The authors present the four energy types as a pyramid: physical at the base, then emotional, then mental, and spiritual at the apex. The pyramid conveys two important ideas. First, hierarchy: neglecting the lower levels undermines the upper ones. Second, integration: peak performance requires all four levels to be simultaneously functional and aligned. Most performance coaching focuses exclusively on the mental and strategic — the top of the pyramid — while ignoring the physical and emotional foundations that make sustained excellence possible.

The Stress-Recovery Oscillation Principle

One of the most scientifically grounded arguments in the book is the principle of oscillation. The authors draw on exercise physiology to make a broader argument: in the body, stress (exertion) plus recovery produces growth and increased capacity. This is the mechanism behind physical training — lift weights, damage muscle fibers slightly, rest and recover, and the body rebuilds stronger. Remove the recovery and you get not growth but injury and burnout.

The authors argue this exact principle applies to all four energy dimensions. Emotional stress plus recovery builds emotional resilience. Mental effort plus rest grows cognitive capacity. Even spiritual energy is deepened by periodic reflection and renewal.

The critical implication is that rest is not laziness — it is part of the performance equation. The problem most driven professionals have is that they treat rest as a failure of will rather than a biological necessity. They push through fatigue, suppress emotions, work through vacations, and then wonder why they feel chronically depleted.

The opposite error is also dangerous: too little stress produces atrophy and under-development. The optimal zone is what the authors call "the performance zone" — a state of appropriate stress that challenges capacity without overwhelming it. Living chronically below this zone (too comfortable, too unstimulated) is just as damaging over time as living chronically above it.

Full Engagement vs. Burnout vs. Rust-Out

The authors identify three zones of existence:

  • Full Engagement: The zone where all four energy systems are appropriately challenged, sustained, and recovered. Performance is high, satisfaction is high, and the person feels alive and effective.
  • Burnout (overtraining): The zone where expenditure chronically exceeds recovery. The person feels exhausted, cynical, emotionally numb, and eventually cannot perform.
  • Rust-Out (undertraining): The zone where too little challenge leads to disengagement, boredom, and atrophy of capacity.

Most performance conversations focus on preventing burnout, but the authors insist rust-out is equally real and damaging. The goal is not simply to avoid burnout but to actively pursue the full engagement zone.

The Ritual Architecture

A major practical framework in the book is the role of rituals — highly deliberate, precisely defined habits that automate the management of energy. The authors make a distinction between willpower-dependent behavior change (which is unreliable and energy-costly) and ritual-based behavior (which runs on autopilot once established, preserving mental and emotional energy for things that matter).

Elite athletes succeed not just because of talent or even training, but because they build elaborate pre-competition rituals that reliably bring them to the optimal performance state. A tennis player might bounce the ball exactly four times before a serve. A golfer might follow an identical pre-shot routine on every shot. These rituals are not superstitious — they are triggers that reliably evoke the desired physical and mental state.

The authors argue that professional knowledge workers should build analogous rituals across all energy dimensions. A morning ritual that moves the body, focuses the mind, and connects the person to their purpose is not self-indulgence — it is high-performance infrastructure. An end-of-workday shutdown ritual that deliberately closes out tasks and creates a mental boundary protects recovery and prevents the bleed of work anxiety into personal life.

The key to ritual is that it must be specific, consistent, and attached to a trigger. "I will be healthier" is a wish. "Every day at 6am before checking email, I walk for twenty minutes" is a ritual with a chance of sticking.

The Purpose Narrative and Personal Mission

The spiritual energy dimension requires its own framework. The authors argue that to access sustained spiritual energy, a person must do the deliberate work of clarifying their deepest values and translating those values into a purpose statement or personal mission. This is not a corporate mission statement exercise — it is a genuine reckoning with who you are, what you believe matters, and what kind of person you want to be remembered as.

They suggest that the most useful entry point into this question is to ask: what would you want the people closest to you to say about you at the end of your life? The answer to that question reveals what actually matters to the person, often in ways that surprise them when compared to how they currently spend their time and energy.

The gap between espoused values (what we say we believe) and enacted values (how we actually behave day to day) is a major source of spiritual energy drain. Reducing that gap is one of the most powerful things a person can do for sustained performance.

3. Major Supporting Examples and Stories

The Corporate Athlete

The authors' foundational analogy is the "Corporate Athlete" — the argument that knowledge workers face performance demands every bit as rigorous as elite athletes, but without any of the training infrastructure athletes take for granted. An athlete has coaches, nutritionists, recovery protocols, and systematic training. A senior executive in a demanding organization has none of these, is expected to perform at peak levels for fifty or sixty hours a week for decades, and is told to treat recovery as a sign of weakness.

One client the authors worked with — a composite figure they call Roger — was a high-achieving executive who was technically delivering results but who was emotionally unavailable to his family, physically deteriorating, and beginning to feel a creeping cynicism about whether his work had any real meaning. On paper, Roger was successful. In full engagement terms, he was running on three cylinders while one was broken.

Working with Roger, the authors helped him see that the physical dimension had to come first — not as a lifestyle choice but as a performance foundation. Roger began sleeping more and exercising consistently. Almost immediately, his emotional reactivity decreased and his mental focus improved. Over time, the authors guided him to examine what he actually valued most and how his daily behavior aligned (or didn't) with those values. The result was not just better performance at work but a qualitatively different experience of his life.

The Sprinter vs. the Marathon Runner

A striking analogy the authors use to explain oscillation is the difference in body type between elite sprinters and elite marathon runners. Sprinters — who engage in pure high-intensity effort followed by complete recovery — develop extraordinary muscle mass and power. Marathon runners — who sustain effort for hours with no recovery — are typically lean almost to the point of fragility. The authors use this to argue that sustained, unrelenting effort without oscillation does not produce power; it produces the minimum efficiency needed for endurance.

The knowledge worker who works sixteen-hour days without breaks or recovery is the marathon runner: functional, but not powerful. The goal is to bring sprinter energy to important work — full intensity during engagement, full recovery between sprints.

The Navy SEAL Training Model

The authors reference the training philosophy of elite military units to illustrate the principle that capacity is built through progressive overloading of the system. Navy SEAL training is deliberately designed to push candidates to and occasionally beyond their limits, then pull back just enough to allow some recovery before pushing again. The result is that candidates who survive develop vastly expanded capacity across physical, emotional, and mental dimensions.

The lesson for ordinary professionals is that strategic discomfort — deliberately choosing activities that push into the zone of productive difficulty — builds capacity over time. Avoiding all discomfort produces stagnation.

4. How the Argument Builds Across the Book

The book follows a purposeful three-part architecture.

Part One establishes the paradigm shift: the enemy is not lack of time but lack of managed energy. The authors dismantle the common approach to performance (work harder, manage time better, be more disciplined) and replace it with the energy management framework. They introduce the four dimensions and the performance pyramid, establishing the theoretical foundation.

Part Two deepens each energy dimension with science, examples, and practical guidance. Physical, emotional, mental, and spiritual energy each get detailed treatment — what depletes them, what replenishes them, how they interact, and what the research shows about high performers' management of each. This is the richest and most actionable section.

Part Three translates the theory into the practical process of transformation. The authors lay out a systematic approach to change: face the truth about current habits, define your purpose, and build rituals that bridge the gap. They are explicit that change is hard because most people try to change behavior through willpower alone, and they explain why the ritual approach is more reliable.

The arc is: diagnosis (we have the problem wrong) → framework (here is the real problem) → solution (here is how to change, with evidence and examples) → transformation process (here is the specific methodology).

5. Honest Critiques and Limitations

Corporate client bias: Almost all the detailed case studies involve high-achieving professionals with the economic resources and schedule flexibility to implement the recommended changes. The framework is significantly harder to apply for someone working multiple jobs with no control over their schedule, no access to gyms, and chronically insufficient sleep due to financial stress rather than poor habits. The book does not adequately acknowledge how privilege shapes the applicability of its prescriptions.

The spiritual energy concept is underdeveloped: While the spiritual energy dimension is conceptually important, the authors' treatment of it is the least rigorous. Clarifying values and writing a purpose statement is good advice, but the book offers relatively thin guidance on how to actually do this deep work, especially for people who find it genuinely difficult to articulate what they value most.

Oversimplification of the stress-recovery model: The oscillation principle is compelling but the authors apply it somewhat loosely across all domains. Physical stress-recovery cycles are well-studied and the physiology is clear. Whether the same model maps perfectly onto emotional or spiritual dimensions is more speculative. The analogy is useful but shouldn't be taken as literal science in those domains.

Light on social and organizational factors: The book is fundamentally individual-centric. It focuses almost entirely on what the individual can do to manage their energy. But much of what depletes people's energy is organizational: toxic management, meaningless work, unfair treatment, and structural overwork. The book largely brackets these structural issues, implicitly placing the burden of performance on the individual. This is a politically convenient framing for corporate clients but doesn't tell the whole story.

Repetition: The book is longer than it needs to be. The core ideas are clear and powerful but are repeated in slightly different forms across multiple chapters, which dilutes the reading experience.

6. How to Apply This

Personal Applications

Audit your energy, not your time. For one week, keep a simple log noting not where your hours went, but how your energy felt at different points — high, medium, low, crashed. Look for patterns. What activities drain each of the four dimensions? What replenishes them?

Treat sleep as performance infrastructure, not a lifestyle choice. Most adults need 7-9 hours. If you're sleeping less than that habitually, you are operating with degraded emotional regulation, reduced cognitive performance, and impaired physical recovery. Protecting sleep is the highest-leverage physical energy intervention available to nearly everyone.

Build one anchor ritual. Pick one daily ritual that replenishes energy in a dimension that is currently depleted. Morning exercise, a midday walk, ten minutes of journaling before bed. Make it specific, tied to a trigger, and non-negotiable for thirty days.

Identify your purpose gap. Write out what you would want people to say about you at your funeral. Then write out what they would probably say based on your actual behavior over the past year. The distance between those two documents is your purpose gap — the source of chronic spiritual energy drain.

Build recovery into your work schedule deliberately. Schedule breaks as firmly as you schedule meetings. After ninety minutes of focused work, take a ten-minute true recovery break (not checking email — something genuinely restorative for you).

Professional Applications

Design the workday around energy cycles, not task lists. Put your most cognitively demanding work during the period of your day when mental energy is naturally highest (for most people, mid-morning). Protect that time ferociously from meetings and administrative tasks.

Create shutdown rituals that genuinely end the workday. The chronic intrusion of work anxiety into non-work time destroys recovery. A deliberate end-of-workday sequence — reviewing tomorrow's priorities, closing applications, physically leaving the workspace — signals the brain that the work cycle is complete and recovery can begin.

Develop emotional self-awareness as a professional skill. Notice how your emotional state affects your interactions, decisions, and creativity. Build the habit of briefly naming your emotional state before important conversations or decisions. Regulated emotional energy is a professional superpower.

Apply oscillation to team design. If you manage people, build in rhythm: intense project sprints followed by genuine lighter periods. Celebrate completion and recovery as much as effort and output. Model the oscillation yourself — your team will match your energy management patterns more than your stated policies.

Reconnect your work to meaning periodically. Quarterly, spend thirty minutes asking: what am I building here that matters beyond the immediate deliverables? Why does this work matter? To whom? This is not a soft exercise — it is how spiritual energy is maintained over a career.

Five Key Takeaways

  1. Energy, not time, is the fundamental currency of high performance. Managing your calendar better without managing your energy will still leave you chronically underperforming. The question is not how to fit more in but how to bring more of yourself to what matters most.

  2. All four energy dimensions — physical, emotional, mental, and spiritual — must be actively managed. Neglecting any one creates deficits that cascade through the others. Physical energy is the non-negotiable foundation; spiritual energy (purpose) is the most powerful sustained motivator.

  3. The body and mind grow through cycles of stress and recovery, not through unrelenting effort. Rest and recovery are not the opposite of performance — they are half of the performance equation. Eliminating recovery eliminates growth.

  4. Rituals — not willpower — are the engine of sustainable behavior change. Designing specific, triggered, consistent habits across all four energy dimensions removes the need to make repeated difficult decisions and preserves higher-order cognitive energy for things that matter.

  5. Living in alignment with your deepest values is not a luxury — it is a performance strategy. The gap between who you say you are and how you actually behave every day is a hidden energy drain. Closing that gap, through deliberate purpose work, is one of the most powerful and underutilized performance levers available.

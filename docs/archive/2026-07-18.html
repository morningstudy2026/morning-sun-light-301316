🏛️ College Athletics Business

1. The August 1 Deadline Is Now Two Weeks Away — Federal Oversight of NIL and Transfers Goes Live

President Trump signed "Urgent National Action to Save College Sports" on April 3, 2026, calling on the NCAA to establish transfer restrictions, eligibility caps, and NIL guardrails by August 1, 2026, backed by the threat of federal funding consequences for noncompliant schools.

The order takes effect August 1 and applies to colleges with at least $20 million in annual athletics revenue, operating on two tracks: pressing the NCAA to update its rules by the deadline, and directing federal agencies to use grant and contract eligibility as the enforcement mechanism.

The executive order will certainly face legal challenges, and many commentators believe its primary purpose is less about directly governing college sports and more about pressuring Congress to pass legislation — particularly the SCORE Act — that would provide the NCAA with an antitrust exemption.

White House Executive Order

2. CSC's NIL Enforcement Ramps Up With $15M in Rejected Deals and Investigations Underway

The College Sports Commission (CSC) issued a formal reminder that all third-party NIL agreements exceeding $600 must flow through the NIL Go platform and reflect legitimate activation of an athlete's name, image, and likeness — and confirmed that investigations are already underway into unreported or non-compliant deals, particularly those tied to transfer portal recruiting.

The CSC has already rejected nearly $15 million in NIL agreements, representing approximately 10% of the total value of deals analyzed since the disclosure requirement was imposed in July 2025.

For universities, collectives, sponsors, and athletes alike, NIL activity is no longer operating in a lightly regulated space — and aggressive oversight may itself become the next frontier of antitrust litigation.

Buchanan Ingersoll & Rooney Analysis

3. Revenue-Share Era One Year In: Power Four Lock In $20M+ Deals While Mid-Majors Diverge

College sports entered a new era on July 1, 2025, the day the House v. NCAA settlement took effect, allowing schools to pay athletes directly for the first time in history.

Schools in the Big 12, Big Ten, and SEC have all confirmed they will pay out the full $20+ million revenue share each season, while the AAC notably is requiring schools to revenue-share $10 million with their athletes over the next three years.

One SEC head coach recently told The Athletic he expects the 2027 baseline to be around $45 million for those expecting to compete in the conference championship and College Football Playoff discussion.

CBS Sports Revenue-Share Explainer

4. NIL Contract Litigation Signals a New Era of Legal Accountability for Athletes and Schools

Duke quarterback Darian Mensah signed a multi-year NIL License Agreement reportedly worth $4 million per year through December 31, 2026, prohibiting him from enrolling at another school — but after announcing his intent to transfer to Miami, Duke sued and won a temporary restraining order barring him from doing so.

Separately, the University of Cincinnati sued former quarterback Brendan Sorsby for breaching his NIL License Agreement by transferring to Texas Tech, seeking $1 million in liquidated damages specified in the contract.

These cases could set important legal or practical precedents for future conduct by schools and student-athletes, with significant uncertainty remaining over which body of law will govern NIL License Agreements.

O'Neil Cannon Legal Analysis

5. China's Kimi K3 AI Model Triggers Global Tech Selloff, Chips Enter Bear Market

Chinese startup Moonshot AI released its new Kimi K3 model on July 17, drawing global attention for its powerful capabilities and sending stock markets reeling — with the Beijing-based company's model delivering performance on industry benchmarks that rivals top-tier offerings from OpenAI and Anthropic.

A selloff in chipmakers gathered pace, driving the high-profile group to a bear market on worries that the AI spending spree is becoming harder to justify, with the cohort of semiconductor powerhouses posting their worst week since April 2025 as a key industry gauge sank 20% from its record.

The surprise breakthrough rippled through global markets, reviving memories of last year's DeepSeek moment and underscoring how assumptions about tomorrow's winners can change almost overnight.

6. US–Iran Conflict Escalates Again, Ceasefire in Doubt as Oil Climbs

The US and Iran traded attacks again on Friday, pressuring oil prices and making a quick return to the fragile ceasefire deal signed last month even less likely — with US Central Command announcing a new round of airstrikes beginning Friday afternoon with the stated goal of "degrading Iranian military capabilities."

Oil extended its weekly advance on the escalating conflict, with West Texas Intermediate rising above $79 a barrel and on track for a weekly gain of more than 11%, while Brent closed near $84.

US inflation remains elevated at 3.5% year-on-year, and Federal Reserve Chairman Kevin Warsh has said the central bank has "no tolerance" for persistently elevated inflation, suggesting rate hikes could still be on the horizon.

Bloomberg: US, Iran Escalate Attacks

7. Netflix Tumbles 10%-Plus After Earnings Miss Investor Expectations on Outlook

Netflix dropped more than 10% after its second-quarter results failed to impress investors, earning 80 cents per share on revenue of $12.56 billion against analyst expectations of 79 cents per share on $12.59 billion.

The company also said it would cut back how often it releases its "What We Watched" engagement reports, which investors rely on to gauge platform performance.

Wall Street is now actively debating Netflix's next growth move after the disappointing outlook.

Bloomberg: AI Selloff Hits Leveraged Funds

8. Judge to Rule by July 22 on States' Bid to Block Paramount–Warner Bros. Mega-Merger

A federal judge said she would rule by July 22 on a request by a dozen states to at least temporarily pause Paramount Skydance Corp.'s $110 billion takeover of Warner Bros. Discovery, with US District Judge Araceli Martínez-Olguín in Oakland setting the timetable after a hearing in the case Friday.

A dozen states sued to stop the studio merger, with twelve state attorneys general filing a lawsuit challenging the acquisition and arguing that the combined Hollywood giant would damage economic competition in the entertainment and media industries.

The Department of Justice had previously announced it would allow the deal to proceed, concluding the merger "is not likely to result in harm to competition or American consumers."

Bloomberg: Paramount–WBD Judge to Rule by July 22

9. Stripe and Advent Make $50B-Plus Bid for PayPal as Fintech M&A Heats Up

PayPal Holdings has been working with advisers to review strategic options, with Stripe and private equity firm Advent pursuing a $50 billion-plus takeover of the fintech pioneer.

Europe is simultaneously fighting to loosen America's grip on payment systems, with concerns over economic sovereignty fueling a search for alternatives to Visa and Mastercard. The convergence of a potential PayPal takeover and Europe's push for payment sovereignty points to an industry inflection point that could redraw the global payments landscape in the months ahead.

Bloomberg: PayPal Works With Goldman, Evercore

10. CalPERS Launches $600 Billion "Total Portfolio" Experiment — Biggest Pension Overhaul in Decades

CalPERS investment chief Stephen Gilmore is breaking down walls between asset classes with a new "total portfolio" investing approach at the nation's largest public pension fund.

CalPERS adopted the Total Portfolio Approach — a system that ditches traditional asset-allocation targets and instead judges every investment on its role within the whole portfolio — with the move set to take effect July 1 to "increase transparency and give staff more flexibility."

Gilmore believes the approach can add 50 to 60 basis points to portfolio returns, a significant figure at a fund of this scale that will be closely watched by pension managers globally.

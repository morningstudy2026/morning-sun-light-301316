1. Protect College Sports Act Awaits Senate Floor Vote — With Graham's Death Complicating the Math

The Senate Commerce Committee advanced the Protect College Sports Act with overwhelming bipartisan support (19–9), sending the legislation to be considered by the full Senate.

Senate Majority Leader Thune was said to "intend" to bring the bill to the floor in July, but the sudden death this weekend of Sen. Lindsey Graham — a key Trump ally — has shrunk the already razor-thin Republican majority at a critical moment. The Senate's scheduled summer recess runs from Aug. 10 through Sept. 11, leaving a narrow window to reach the 60 votes needed for passage.

Senate Commerce Committee — PCSA

2. Big Ten and SEC Remain in Opposition to the PCSA Over NIL "Associated Entities" Loophole

The two most powerful conferences in college football remain opposed; in a joint statement, the Big Ten and SEC said that despite "sustained engagement and good faith efforts," their critical revisions to the bill had not been accepted. At the heart of the dispute is a provision that would count multimedia-rights and sponsor NIL deals — the primary tool schools use to exceed the revenue-sharing cap — against the school's overall compensation limit. For a year now, schools have used multimedia rights partners, corporate sponsors, and apparel brands to intentionally circumvent the cap, redirecting revenue to rosters through manufactured NIL opportunities in a hotly competitive recruiting environment.

CBS Sports — Big Ten/SEC Opposition

3. CSC Releases NIL Data Report — Compliance Gaps Still Wide

On July 8, 2026, the College Sports Commission released its NIL Data Report, highlighting third-party NIL activity across Division I athletics from May and June 2026, as well as cumulative figures since NIL Go's launch. The underlying numbers paint a sobering picture of enforcement: the CSC has approved nearly $300 million in third-party athlete NIL deals since its inception, while more than $200 million in additional compensation has either been rejected or remains under review — a majority in both categories believed to involve associated entities.

NIL Newsstand — CSC Data Report

4. NCAA's New Age-Based Eligibility Model Begins Reshaping Roster Strategy

In June 2026, the NCAA adopted a new age-based eligibility model that significantly alters roster management and athlete compensation planning; under the new framework, Division I athletes generally receive five years of eligibility without traditional redshirts, with the eligibility clock beginning at initial full-time collegiate enrollment or the academic year following an athlete's nineteenth birthday, whichever occurs first.

Although the long-term impact remains uncertain, the rule may have significant implications for recruiting, transfer portal activity, roster construction, and revenue-sharing decisions, as institutions may increasingly evaluate athlete compensation through the lens of a five-year participation window.

5. 2026 Conference Realignment Takes Effect — Pac-12 Reborn, FCS Powers Move Up

Unlike the seismic 2024 shuffle, the Power Four leagues stood pat; all of the movement this time is a Group of Five story, headlined by the rebuilt Pac-12 and a chain of Mountain West, Conference USA, Sun Belt, and MAC moves, plus two programs jumping up from the FCS.

The Pac-12 rebuilt to eight teams by adding Boise State, Colorado State, Fresno State, San Diego State, Utah State (from the Mountain West), and Texas State (from the Sun Belt), joining holdovers Oregon State and Washington State.

North Dakota State and Sacramento State both reclassified from the FCS to the FBS for 2026.

Lindsey Graham, the South Carolina senior senator and foreign policy hawk, died Saturday — he was 71, and the cause of death was described as "a brief and sudden illness."

The purchase price is 59% higher than the previous record of $6.05 billion set when Josh Harris purchased the Washington Commanders in July 2023.

Intel Corp. is spending €5 billion ($5.7 billion) to expand its plant in Ireland as the chipmaker attempts to regain manufacturing dominance for the AI boom, with the investment expanding capacity at the Leixlip campus on the outskirts of Dublin as part of Intel's drive to increase output of data center processors.

The investment will add several hundred jobs to the 4,900 people Intel currently employs in Ireland, with most of the funds targeted for deployment by end-2027, representing about 30% of Intel's $17 billion planned capital expenditure for 2026.

II · Today's Book

The Laws of Subtraction

by Matthew E. May

The Laws of Subtraction: A Detailed Key-Ideas Brief

Central Thesis and Why It Matters

Matthew E. May's central argument is deceptively simple yet profoundly countercultural: the most powerful creative and problem-solving act available to human beings is removal, not addition. We live and work inside a civilization that treats accumulation as progress — more features, more steps, more words, more meetings, more options, more complexity. May argues that this instinct is not only counterproductive but actively hostile to clarity, elegance, beauty, and effectiveness.

The book's core claim is that subtraction — the deliberate, disciplined act of leaving things out — is the engine behind every work, product, experience, and idea that people describe as elegant or brilliant. What looks like inspired genius in retrospect is almost always an act of ruthless editing. The question "What can we add?" is the wrong starting question. The right question is "What can we remove, reduce, restrict, or refuse?"

Why does this matter beyond aesthetics? May makes a practical case: in a world of information overload, choice overload, and organizational complexity, the organizations and individuals who master subtraction gain competitive and creative advantages that cannot be easily replicated by people still adding. Simplicity is not a natural state — it is an achievement. The default is always more. Going against that default requires discipline, skill, and a specific set of mental frameworks.

The book's intellectual lineage draws on cognitive psychology (the paradox of choice, cognitive load theory), design thinking, Zen philosophy, business strategy, and neuroscience. May synthesizes these into six distinct "laws," each representing a domain where subtraction creates value.

The Six Laws: Each Framework Fully Explained

Law 1: What Isn't There Can Matter More Than What Is

The first law establishes the foundational principle of the entire book. May argues that absence, emptiness, and negative space carry meaning and power that positive elements — things actually present — cannot carry. This is not mystical; it is observable in every elegant design, every great piece of music, and every memorable story.

The logic draws on the concept of negative space from visual art and design. A painting is not only about what is painted; it is equally about what the painter chose not to paint. The empty space around a focal object gives that object definition, weight, and presence. Remove the space and you remove the power. The same principle applies to writing (a sentence becomes powerful through what is not said), to music (the silence between notes is where tension lives), to architecture (an empty room invites contemplation in ways a cluttered room never can), and to business communication (a clear, spare message penetrates where an overloaded one bounces off).

May uses this law to challenge the assumption that completeness is a virtue. A complete argument, a complete feature set, a complete explanation — these are often artistic and practical failures because they leave no room for the audience, user, or reader to participate, to project meaning, to engage. The very act of leaving something out invites the other person in. Incompleteness, strategically deployed, creates engagement.

Law 2: The Simplest Rules Create the Most Effective Order

The second law addresses systems — social, organizational, biological, technological — and argues that the most functional and adaptive systems operate on the smallest number of governing rules rather than elaborate regulatory frameworks.

The logic here is partly drawn from complexity theory and emergent behavior. Bird flocking, for instance, produces staggeringly beautiful and coordinated behavior from just three simple rules about individual bird movement. No central coordinator, no complex instruction manual — just minimal rules producing complex, elegant order. May applies this insight to organizational design: companies that try to govern every situation with detailed policies produce paralysis, resentment, and gaming of the rules. Companies that operate on a small number of clear, principle-based rules produce creativity, adaptability, and ownership.

The framework argues that the challenge for any designer of systems — whether a parent, a manager, a software architect, or a policymaker — is to identify the irreducible minimum set of rules that will produce the desired emergent behavior. This requires deep understanding of the goal, because you cannot simplify intelligently without knowing what matters most. Simplifying rules is not lazy; it is a high-cognitive-effort act of distillation.

Law 3: Limiting Conditions Spur Creative Solutions

The third law is perhaps the most counterintuitive and practically liberating: constraints do not inhibit creativity; they generate it. Absolute freedom is the enemy of creative achievement. The blank page terrifies. The open brief paralyzes. The infinite budget corrupts.

May's argument draws on substantial research in cognitive psychology and creative practice. When people are given unlimited resources and complete freedom, they produce mediocre, unfocused work. When given tight constraints — a word limit, a budget ceiling, a deadline, a specific problem definition — they produce focused, inventive, surprising work. Constraints force the brain to abandon the obvious paths (which are the first casualties of restriction) and find lateral routes that would never have been discovered under conditions of abundance.

The practical implication is radical: if you want more creativity from yourself or your team, add constraints deliberately. Impose a budget that is uncomfortable. Set a word count that forces cutting. Define the problem more narrowly. Remove options. The counterintuitive management move is not to give people more freedom but to give them better-designed restrictions.

This law also addresses the psychology of constraint-reception: people initially experience constraints as frustrating obstacles. The skill is in reframing constraints as creative invitations — the wall that forces you to find the door.

Law 4: Breakthrough Thinking Occurs in the Space Between

The fourth law is epistemological — it concerns how genuine insight actually happens. May argues that new ideas do not emerge from continuous, effortful, focused thinking. They emerge in the pauses, the transitions, the liminal spaces between periods of intense focus.

The supporting argument comes from neuroscience: the default mode network of the brain — the system that activates during apparent rest, daydreaming, and unfocused attention — is now understood to be a creative integration engine. It is during these periods that the brain connects disparate pieces of information across memory systems in ways that directed, focused attention cannot. This is the neurological basis of the shower epiphany, the insight that comes on a walk, the solution that arrives at 3 a.m.

The practical implication is that busyness — the cultural ideal of constant productivity and perpetual connectivity — is the enemy of breakthrough thinking. Scheduling margin, protecting time for deliberate unstructured thinking, and creating transitions between focused work periods are not luxuries but necessities for anyone who needs to generate genuinely new ideas rather than just execute on existing ones.

May connects this to the concept of "Ma" from Japanese aesthetics — the productive pause, the meaningful interval. Not emptiness as absence of value, but emptiness as the condition for value to arise.

Law 5: Engaging People Is About Doing Less and Requiring More

The fifth law addresses communication, education, leadership, and experience design. The argument is that the most powerful communications and experiences are those that create active participation rather than passive reception — and that achieving this requires the communicator or designer to deliberately do less.

The logic: when you explain everything, you leave nothing for the audience to discover. Discovery is the most engaging and memorable mode of understanding. A message that requires the listener to complete a thought, draw an inference, or fill a gap is a message that the listener actively owns. A message that delivers every conclusion prearranged requires nothing and produces nothing lasting.

This law has immediate applications in teaching (Socratic questioning versus lecturing), in leadership (asking rather than telling), in marketing (campaigns that provoke curiosity rather than state claims), and in product design (products that reward exploration rather than providing exhaustive manuals). The art is in knowing precisely how much to withhold — enough to invite engagement, not so much as to produce confusion.

Law 6: Doing Something Isn't Always Better Than Doing Nothing

The sixth law is the most philosophically challenging because it attacks one of the deepest imperatives of modern professional culture: the bias toward action. May argues that in many situations, the wisest, most effective, and most courageous response is deliberate inaction — and that the pressure to always act, always respond, always produce is itself a form of noise that blocks clear judgment.

The argument distinguishes between two kinds of doing nothing: passive avoidance (procrastination, fear-based paralysis) and active restraint (disciplined refusal to act until the right action is clear, or recognition that no action is required). The second is what May advocates. It requires greater confidence and clarity than acting does, because acting always feels productive even when it makes things worse.

This law has deep resonance in medicine (overtesting and overtreatment as sources of harm), in organizational life (the meeting that should not have happened, the policy that should not have been written, the project that should not have been launched), and in personal decision-making (the relationship not pursued, the commitment not made).

Major Supporting Examples and Stories

Steve Jobs and the iPod's Absent Buttons: May explores how Jobs's obsessive removal of features — most dramatically, the iPod's reduction to a single click wheel with no visible keyboard — was not a design aesthetic but a design argument: every additional element is a decision burden placed on the user. Removal is an act of respect for the user's attention.

The 3M Post-it as Constraint Triumph: The Post-it note emerged not from a search for a breakthrough adhesive but from the accidental discovery of an adhesive that failed in its original purpose — it didn't stick permanently. Rather than discarding the failure, a scientist reimagined the constraint (temporary sticking) as the product's central feature. The limitation became the value proposition.

The Empty Seat at Pixar Meetings: May describes how Ed Catmull at Pixar reportedly maintained the practice of leaving an empty chair at important meetings — symbolically representing the absent perspective, the unconsidered stakeholder, the user not in the room. The visual absence created a kind of mental presence that shaped decisions.

Twitter's Character Limit: The 140-character limit on Twitter was initially seen as a technical constraint born of SMS infrastructure. May frames it as an accidental act of subtraction that became the platform's core creative engine, forcing users into a discipline of compression that produced a new form of public writing.

The Shaker Furniture Tradition: May returns repeatedly to Shaker design philosophy, encapsulated in their principle that an object should be as simple as it can be while doing its job perfectly. Shaker craftspeople would systematically remove every decorative element that did not serve function, producing pieces of stunning beauty through radical discipline. May uses this as an enduring metaphor for the entire book's argument.

Hemingway's Iceberg Theory: The writer's famous principle — that the power of a story comes from what is left beneath the surface, known by the author but not stated — is used to illustrate Law 1. The reader's experience of depth and resonance comes precisely from what Hemingway chose not to write.

How the Argument Builds Across the Book

May structures the book so that each law reinforces and extends the others rather than standing in isolation. Laws 1 and 2 establish the philosophical foundation: absence has power, and simplicity generates order. These two laws challenge the reader's default assumptions at the most basic level.

Laws 3 and 4 move from philosophy to psychology, showing how the mind actually responds to less — creatively in the case of constraints, insightfully in the case of unstructured space. These chapters give the reader the cognitive mechanism behind why subtraction works.

Laws 5 and 6 are the practical and behavioral culminations, asking the reader to change how they communicate, teach, lead, and decide. By the time the reader reaches these laws, the philosophical and psychological groundwork has been laid that makes these behavioral prescriptions feel logical rather than arbitrary.

The cumulative effect is a coherent worldview: subtraction is not a technique but a discipline, not a style but a philosophy, applicable at every level from a single sentence to an entire organization. The book builds toward the position that learning to subtract well is one of the highest-order skills available to a modern person or organization.

Honest Critiques and Limitations

The Survivorship Problem: May's examples are overwhelmingly drawn from celebrated successes — Apple, Pixar, iconic design objects. We do not see the cases where subtraction went wrong: the feature that was removed and users missed badly, the constraint that produced not creativity but frustration, the inaction that allowed a problem to compound. The argument would be more robust if it engaged with failure cases.

The Operationalization Gap: The book is philosophically compelling but practically underspecified. The laws tell you that you should subtract, why subtraction works, and what subtraction looks like when successful. They are less helpful in telling you exactly how to identify what to remove in a specific situation. The skill of discernment — knowing which thing to cut — is the hard part, and it receives relatively little systematic treatment.

Selection Bias in Examples: Many of the book's cases are from design-intensive industries (technology, consumer products, arts) where aesthetic simplicity is already a valued dimension of competition. The argument may transfer less cleanly to industries like regulatory compliance, complex engineering, or social services, where adding is sometimes genuinely necessary and the cost of removal can be serious harm.

The Privilege of Simplicity: May does not engage with the critique that simplicity is sometimes a luxury available only to those who can afford to remove — that organizations operating under genuine resource constraints sometimes add precisely because they cannot afford redundancy, and that the choice to strip things down is itself a form of power not universally available.

The Risk of Misapplication: The sixth law — that doing nothing is sometimes better than acting — could be misread as a justification for avoidance or passivity. May distinguishes between these, but the distinction is philosophically clear and practically difficult, particularly for people whose natural mode is already avoidant rather than action-biased.

How to Apply This: Personal and Professional Applications

Personal Applications

Edit your communication ruthlessly. Before sending any important email, message, or document, read it once looking only for what to remove. Ask: does this sentence do work, or is it restating something already said? Does this paragraph serve the reader or my own need to feel thorough? Cut anything that does not actively contribute.

Design constraints into your creative work. If you're working on a project that feels stuck or uninspired, do not add resources — add a restriction. Give yourself half the time, half the words, half the budget. Watch what happens to your focus and inventiveness when you cannot take the easy path.

Protect unstructured time. Schedule blank time in your week — not meetings, not tasks, not even reading. Walks, showers, sitting quietly. Treat this as non-negotiable work time, because for the integrative and generative thinking you actually need, it is.

Practice strategic withholding in conversations. In your next important conversation — teaching a concept, coaching someone, influencing a decision — resist the urge to make every point. Make one or two and leave room for the other person to respond, question, and fill gaps. Notice the difference in their engagement.

Professional Applications

Audit organizational rules and policies. Identify three to five policies in your team or organization that exist primarily because something once went wrong, not because they serve a current need. Challenge whether each one should be replaced with a single, more encompassing principle. Present the simplification as an act of trust rather than an act of laziness.

Introduce constraints as creative tools. In your next brainstorming session or project kickoff, deliberately narrow the brief. Specify a constraint that seems uncomfortably tight. Frame it as an invitation: "We're going to solve this without using [the obvious resource/approach]."

Build negative space into your team's calendar. Protect one meeting-free period per week for your team. Not for individual work tasks — for thinking. Watch whether this produces ideas that scheduled meetings never do.

Evaluate every proposed new initiative through a subtraction lens. Before approving any new project, new feature, or new process, ask what existing commitment must be removed to make room for it. Refuse to add without a corresponding removal. This forces organizational prioritization and prevents the accumulation of organizational complexity.

Redesign your presentations around absence. Remove every slide that does not carry the full weight of the argument. Remove every bullet point that repeats what you will say in words. Aim for the minimum number of slides that conveys the maximum meaning. Measure success not by completeness but by clarity.

Five Key Takeaways

  1. Subtraction is the most underused creative and strategic tool. The default human bias is toward addition, which means the greatest opportunities for clarity, elegance, and effectiveness are almost always found by going against that default and asking what can be removed.

  2. Constraints generate creativity; they do not block it. If you want more innovative thinking from yourself or your team, the counterintuitive move is to impose tighter restrictions, not to offer greater freedom or more resources.

  3. What is absent carries meaning. Empty space, unfinished statements, and deliberate gaps invite engagement, participation, and discovery in ways that complete, overloaded communication never can. Strategic absence is an active design choice.

  4. Breakthrough thinking lives in the gaps. The pauses, transitions, and unstructured intervals in your schedule are not wasted time — they are the conditions under which genuine insight occurs. Protect them as fiercely as you protect focused work time.

  5. Doing less is often the most demanding and courageous choice. Inaction, editing, refusal, and restraint require greater clarity and confidence than action does, because acting always feels productive. The discipline to subtract — to stop, to remove, to refuse — is a skill that must be deliberately cultivated against strong cultural and psychological pressure in the opposite direction.
